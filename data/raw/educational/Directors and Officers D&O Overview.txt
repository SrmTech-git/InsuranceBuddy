Directors and Officers (D&O) Liability Insurance
=================================================

This document explains what D&O insurance is, the three-side
structure that makes it distinctive, what kinds of claims it
responds to, how public-company D&O differs from private-company
and nonprofit D&O, and the typical exclusions and gaps.


WHAT D&O COVERS
================

D&O liability insurance covers directors and officers personally,
and (often) the entity itself, for claims alleging "wrongful
acts" in their capacity as directors or officers. The classic
claim: a shareholder, regulator, employee, or creditor alleges
that a board or executive team breached its fiduciary duty,
mismanaged the company, made false disclosures, or otherwise
caused harm through a governance failure.

Why D&O exists:
Directors and officers face personal liability for their
corporate roles. Corporate indemnification (the company
agreeing to defend and indemnify) may be unavailable when the
company is insolvent, when state law prohibits indemnification
for certain claims, or when the company itself is the
plaintiff. D&O insurance fills the gap by responding directly
to the individual D&O.

What "wrongful act" means:
Defined broadly in most policies — any actual or alleged act,
error, omission, misstatement, misleading statement, neglect,
or breach of duty in the insured's capacity as a director or
officer. The breadth of "wrongful act" is one of D&O's
distinguishing features.


THE THREE-SIDE STRUCTURE — A, B, C
====================================

D&O policies are structured around three "sides" of coverage,
each addressing a different scenario.

Side A — Direct coverage for individual D&Os:
Pays the individual director or officer when the company has
NOT indemnified them (because it can't — insolvency, legal
prohibition, refused to indemnify, etc.). Side A is
non-rescindable in most modern policies, meaning the carrier
can't void coverage for fraud committed by other insureds —
critical for innocent D&Os.

Side A is the personal protection layer. For many directors,
the Side A piece is the most important — it's what stands
between them and personal asset exposure when the company
can't help.

Side B — Reimbursement of the company:
Reimburses the company when the company HAS indemnified its
directors and officers as permitted by law and bylaws. The
company pays the indemnification; the policy reimburses the
company.

Side B is the most heavily used side in practice — most D&O
claims involve company-indemnified D&Os, and the company
recovers from the policy.

Side C — Entity coverage:
Covers the company itself (the "entity") for claims against
the entity. For public companies, Side C is typically
LIMITED to securities claims — claims under federal or state
securities law (10b-5 claims, Section 11 claims, etc.). For
private companies and nonprofits, Side C is often broader,
covering any claim against the entity (subject to
exclusions).

Why public-company Side C is limited:
If Side C covered all entity claims for a public company,
the carrier would essentially be writing commercial general
liability and other lines under the D&O policy, which would
explode pricing. The narrow Side C focuses D&O on the
distinctive entity exposure — securities lawsuits — that
other policies don't cover.

Side A-only / Difference-in-Conditions (DIC) policies:
Excess policies sitting above the main D&O program,
providing additional Side A protection for individual D&Os.
DIC policies "drop down" to fill gaps when the underlying
policy doesn't respond (carrier insolvency, certain
exclusions, etc.) — providing extra layers of personal
protection for board members.


CLAIMS-MADE COVERAGE
=====================

Like E&O, D&O is universally claims-made. The same
retroactive date / tail coverage mechanics apply:

Retroactive date:
Acts before the retro date aren't covered.

Extended Reporting Period:
When the policy terminates, ERP / tail extends the reporting
window. Critical when a company is sold, goes private, or
winds down — the predecessor company's D&Os may face claims
years after termination.

Run-off coverage:
A specific form of tail coverage purchased when a company is
acquired or merged out of existence. The run-off policy
provides extended reporting for the predecessor entity and
its former D&Os, typically for 6 years (matching common
statute of limitations on derivative claims). Run-off is
often negotiated as part of the M&A transaction and may be
mandatory under the merger agreement.


WHO IS INSURED
==============

The basic insured definition:
- Directors and officers (current, former, and future during
  the policy period)
- Often: employees, in their capacity as directors or
  officers when acting on behalf of the company
- Often: spouses, domestic partners, estates, and heirs of
  insureds (protecting personal assets)
- Often: outside directors of subsidiaries and affiliated
  entities (subsidiary coverage)

"Outside Directorship Liability" (ODL):
Coverage extension for individuals who serve on outside
boards at the company's direction or with company consent.
Common for executives serving on nonprofit boards, trade
association boards, or boards of portfolio companies. ODL
usually applies excess of any other insurance available
through the outside entity.


COMMON D&O CLAIMS
==================

Securities class actions:
The most prominent D&O claim type. Shareholders allege the
company and its officers made false or misleading statements
about the company's prospects, financial condition, or
material events, causing the stock to drop when truth
emerged. Filed under 10b-5, Section 11, or state securities
law. The bread and butter of public-company D&O.

Derivative suits:
Shareholders sue in the name of the company against
directors/officers, alleging breach of fiduciary duty caused
harm to the company. Procedurally complex; often follows a
securities class action or merger.

Merger objection suits:
Shareholders of the target challenge the merger price,
process, or disclosures. Once nearly automatic on any public
M&A, these have shifted from class actions to disclosure-
only settlements in recent years.

Regulatory investigations and enforcement:
SEC investigations, DOJ inquiries, state AG actions, FTC
investigations. D&O often covers defense costs even before
a formal claim is made — "informal investigation" coverage
varies by policy.

Bankruptcy-related claims:
When a company fails, creditors and bankruptcy trustees often
sue former directors and officers alleging breach of duty,
fraudulent transfer, or deepening insolvency. Some of the
largest D&O claims arise in bankruptcy.

Employment-related claims by senior executives:
Wrongful termination, discrimination, or retaliation by
former officers can be claims against the company AND against
the directors who voted for the termination. Often overlaps
with EPLI; coordination of coverage matters.

Government contractor claims:
False Claims Act suits, contract violation allegations
against government contractors implicate D&O for officers
and directors.

Cyber-related D&O:
Increasingly, breach incidents trigger derivative or
shareholder suits against directors alleging failure of
oversight on cybersecurity. Cyber-specific exclusions in
D&O policies are a focus area.


PUBLIC vs PRIVATE vs NONPROFIT D&O
====================================

The three markets differ substantially:

Public-company D&O:
- Dominant claim type: securities class actions
- High limits common ($10M to $100M+ programs)
- Narrow Side C (securities-only)
- High pricing relative to private
- Heavy underwriting on financials, MD&A disclosures, stock
  volatility, recent M&A activity
- "Going public" D&O — separate policy structure for IPO
  exposures (Section 11 risk)

Private-company D&O:
- Dominant claim types: employment-related claims (often
  overlaps with EPLI), commercial disputes, creditor claims,
  M&A claims
- Lower limits typical ($1M-$10M programs)
- Broader Side C (covers entity claims, not just securities)
- Often packaged with EPLI, fiduciary, and crime in a
  "private company management liability" package
- Pricing more correlated with employee count and revenue
  than financial complexity

Nonprofit D&O:
- Dominant claim types: employment-related, regulatory,
  donor disputes, fiduciary breach
- Modest limits typical ($1M-$5M)
- Often packaged with EPLI for nonprofit organizations
- Volunteer Protection Act provides some statutory immunity
  to volunteer directors of qualifying nonprofits, but D&O
  is still important for defense costs and exceptions to
  immunity
- Lower pricing than commercial private


COMMON D&O EXCLUSIONS
======================

Conduct exclusions (the "moral hazard" exclusions):
- Fraud / dishonesty: typically requires a "final adjudication"
  in the underlying matter before the exclusion bites —
  meaning the carrier defends until proven fraud, protecting
  innocent defense costs
- Personal profit / illegal remuneration: similar final-
  adjudication trigger
- Severability: only the wrongdoer is excluded — innocent
  D&Os retain coverage

Insured vs insured exclusion:
Bars claims by one insured against another. Designed to
prevent collusive suits (the company suing its own directors
to recover from the D&O policy). Common carve-backs:
- Bankruptcy trustee suits (so creditor recovery isn't
  blocked)
- Whistleblower claims
- Employment claims
- Derivative suits brought independently by shareholders

Prior acts exclusion:
Acts before the retro date.

Prior and pending litigation exclusion:
Matters pending or threatened before the policy inception.

Bodily injury / property damage:
Belongs on CGL.

Professional services:
Belongs on E&O. (Some policies provide partial professional
services coverage as a buy-back.)

ERISA / employee benefits:
Belongs on Fiduciary liability policy.

Antitrust:
Often excluded outright; sometimes covered with sub-limits or
endorsement.

Pollution:
Belongs on Environmental policy.

Wage and hour claims:
Often excluded or sub-limited even in employment-related
coverage extensions.

Specific named matters:
Carriers often exclude particular pending or threatened
matters identified at underwriting (a known investigation, a
threatened derivative suit).


KEY POLICY STRUCTURE FEATURES
==============================

Defense in or outside limits:
D&O defense is typically WITHIN limits — defense costs erode
what's available to pay claims. Eroding limits is a major
consideration on long-running securities cases where defense
can run tens of millions before any settlement.

Allocation:
When a claim involves both covered and uncovered matters, or
both insured and non-insured parties (the company as a
non-insured in some private policies), the carrier and
insured must "allocate" defense costs and settlements between
covered and non-covered portions. Modern policies often
provide pre-set allocation percentages for securities cases
favorable to insureds.

Order of payments:
Side A claims have payment priority — the policy is designed
to protect individual D&Os first when the entity is
insolvent. The "order of payments" provision sets out who
gets paid first when multiple claims are pending.

Severability:
For both the application (one insured's misrepresentation
doesn't void coverage for innocent insureds) and exclusions
(only the wrongdoer is excluded for conduct exclusions).
Severability provisions are heavily negotiated in larger
placements.

Non-rescindability:
Modern Side A coverage is often non-rescindable — the
carrier can't void coverage for application fraud committed
by other insureds. Critical for individual D&Os relying on
Side A.


THE D&O PROGRAM STRUCTURE
==========================

Larger companies typically build D&O coverage as a tower:

- Primary policy (typically $10M-$25M): the first layer,
  carries the most coverage detail and exclusions; sets the
  template for following form policies above
- Excess policies (each $10M-$25M+): "follow form" the
  primary, adding limits
- Side A DIC policies on top of or alongside the tower:
  additional Side A capacity that drops down to fill gaps in
  the main program

A typical large-public-company program might be $50M-$200M
of total D&O limits across 5-15 carriers, with Side A DIC
adding additional protection.


COMMON ADJACENT COVERAGES
==========================

D&O is part of a broader "management liability" suite:

- Employment Practices Liability (EPLI) — employment claims
- Fiduciary Liability — ERISA / employee benefits plan
  governance
- Crime / Fidelity — employee theft, social engineering
- Kidnap and Ransom (K&R) — for international exposures
- Cyber Liability — privacy and security breaches
- Representations and Warranties (R&W) Insurance — for M&A
  transactions

Many carriers package private-company D&O with EPLI,
Fiduciary, and Crime in a single "management liability"
form. Public-company D&O is almost always written as a
standalone policy.


KEY TAKEAWAYS
=============

- D&O covers directors and officers personally (Side A), the
  company's reimbursement of indemnified D&Os (Side B), and
  the company itself for certain claims (Side C)
- Public-company Side C is securities-only; private and
  nonprofit Side C is broader
- Claims-made structure means retroactive date and tail
  coverage are critical, especially on M&A transactions
- Conduct exclusions typically have "final adjudication"
  triggers preserving defense costs
- Insured vs insured exclusion bars internal claims with
  important carve-backs
- D&O sits alongside EPLI, Fiduciary, Crime, and Cyber in a
  management liability program
