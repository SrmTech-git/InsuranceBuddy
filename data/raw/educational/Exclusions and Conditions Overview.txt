Exclusions and Conditions: The Other Two Pillars of a Policy
=============================================================

This document explains the two policy components that sit
alongside the coverage form and endorsements — exclusions
(what's NOT covered) and conditions (what the insured has to
DO for coverage to apply). It's a conceptual companion to the
Endorsements Overview; together those three components — base
form, endorsements, exclusions, and conditions — make up the
full insurance contract.

This doc is intentionally written without form numbers so it
serves general "how do exclusions work" / "what is a condition"
queries broadly.


THE FULL POLICY ANATOMY
========================

An insurance policy is built from layered components:

- Declarations: the cover sheet — named insured, policy
  period, limits, premium, list of attached forms and
  endorsements
- Insuring Agreement: the carrier's promise to pay for a
  defined category of loss (the affirmative coverage grant)
- Definitions: terms with policy-specific meanings
- Exclusions: what the policy does NOT cover
- Conditions: rules and duties governing how coverage works
- Endorsements: amendments modifying any of the above

Exclusions and conditions are sometimes overlooked because
they don't grant coverage — but they're often what determines
whether a claim actually gets paid. The insuring agreement
opens the door; exclusions close specific doors; conditions
say what the insured has to do to walk through.


PART ONE — EXCLUSIONS
======================


WHAT AN EXCLUSION IS
====================

An exclusion is a provision that removes coverage the
insuring agreement would otherwise provide. Exclusions narrow
the affirmative coverage grant — they don't grant any
coverage, only take it away.

Reading order matters:
1. Insuring agreement establishes what the policy covers in
   principle
2. Definitions clarify the scope of key terms
3. Exclusions remove specific categories from that coverage
4. Endorsements may add coverage back (an "exception" or
   "carve-back" to an exclusion) or add further exclusions

A claim is covered only if it falls within the insuring
agreement AND no exclusion applies. The carrier bears the
burden of proving an exclusion applies; the insured bears the
burden of proving the claim falls within the insuring
agreement.


WHY EXCLUSIONS EXIST
====================

Carriers use exclusions for several reasons:

Risk that doesn't fit the line:
A general liability policy isn't designed to cover auto
exposures — those belong on commercial auto. The CGL
auto exclusion isn't denying coverage maliciously; it's
keeping the line in its lane.

Risk that's separately insured:
Workers compensation exposures go on WC, not on CGL or EPLI.
Professional services exposures go on E&O, not CGL.
Excluding these from one line directs them to the right
line.

Catastrophic risk requiring separate underwriting:
Flood, earthquake, war, nuclear — these are excluded because
their potential aggregate exposure would destabilize a normal
carrier's book. Either they go uncovered, get covered by
specialty markets (NFIP for flood), or get added back by
specific endorsement with separate underwriting.

Risk that's uninsurable as a matter of public policy:
Intentional acts, criminal acts, punitive damages in some
states — public policy prohibits insuring these because
insuring them would undermine the deterrent function of
liability.

Risk the underwriter declines on this specific account:
Account-specific exclusions added by endorsement during
underwriting. A contractor with a history of mold claims
might have a mold exclusion added; a restaurant with prior
assault and battery claims might have an A&B exclusion.

Adverse selection management:
Wear and tear, intentional damage, and similar exclusions
prevent the policy from becoming a maintenance contract —
insurance covers fortuitous losses, not predictable
deterioration.


TYPES OF EXCLUSIONS
====================

Absolute exclusions:
Remove coverage with no carve-backs. The excluded category is
simply not covered, period. Examples: nuclear hazard exclusion
on most policies, intentional act exclusions for most
purposes.

Exclusions with exceptions ("carve-backs"):
Remove coverage but specify situations where the exclusion
doesn't apply, restoring some coverage. The exclusion narrows
the original grant; the carve-back partially restores it.
Most common exclusions have at least some carve-backs.

Example: a typical CGL pollution exclusion excludes pollution
liability broadly, but the carve-back may preserve coverage
for hostile fire, building heating equipment, and certain
specific named situations. Reading the carve-backs is as
important as reading the exclusion.

Conditional exclusions:
Exclude coverage if a stated condition is met. Example: an
exclusion for property damage to property in the insured's
care, custody, or control — applies when the property is
"in the insured's care, custody, or control" but not
otherwise.

Named-peril vs all-peril exclusions:
Property forms can be written named-perils (covers only listed
causes) or open-perils (covers all causes except those
excluded). In open-perils forms, the exclusions do the work
of defining coverage scope. In named-perils forms, exclusions
play a more limited role because most causes are already
outside the affirmative grant.

Personal/separate vs property/concurrent causation:
When two causes contribute to a loss — one covered, one
excluded — different policies handle the question differently.
Anti-concurrent causation clauses say if any excluded cause
contributes, the loss is excluded; some courts disregard such
clauses. The interaction between covered and excluded causes
is one of the most heavily litigated areas in property
insurance.


COMMON EXCLUSIONS ACROSS LINES
================================

Some exclusions appear in nearly every policy:

War and military action:
Excludes losses from war, civil war, insurrection, and
related military hostilities. Heavily litigated for cyber
events attributed to nation-states.

Nuclear hazard:
Excludes losses from nuclear reaction, radiation, or
radioactive contamination. A holdover from Cold War-era
underwriting concerns; nuclear losses are addressed through
specialized pools.

Intentional acts:
Excludes losses the insured intentionally caused or expected.
Limits insurance to fortuitous losses. Severability provisions
typically preserve coverage for innocent co-insureds even
when one insured acted intentionally.

Criminal acts:
Excludes acts that constitute a crime. Sometimes overlaps
with intentional acts; sometimes broader (a negligent crime
like negligent homicide).

Wear and tear / inherent vice / latent defect:
Excludes deterioration and gradual breakdown. Insurance
covers sudden fortuitous losses, not maintenance.

Mechanical breakdown:
Excludes losses from mechanical failure of equipment.
Equipment Breakdown coverage is a separate line or
endorsement.

Pollution:
Excludes pollution liability. Common in CGL and property.
Pollution coverage is a separate environmental insurance
market.

Asbestos, lead, mold, silica:
Often excluded due to mass tort exposure. Coverage may be
restored with sub-limits via endorsement; specialty markets
provide standalone coverage.

Communicable disease:
Added widely after COVID-19 disrupted business interruption
markets. Specifically excludes losses from communicable
disease and pandemic.

Cyber / data breach:
Most property and CGL policies have explicit cyber exclusions
directing those exposures to cyber liability policies.

Professional services:
On CGL, excludes liability for professional services
(directing those to E&O). On property, excludes loss to
property arising from professional services.

Employment practices:
On CGL, excludes employment-related practices (directing
those to EPLI).

Auto:
On CGL, excludes auto liability (directing to commercial
auto). On property, may exclude damage to vehicles.

Aircraft and watercraft:
Often excluded above a threshold size on personal and
commercial property policies — these have specialty
markets.

Earth movement / earthquake:
Excluded from most property policies. Separate earthquake
endorsement or specialty policy adds coverage.

Flood:
Excluded from virtually all property policies. NFIP or
private flood is required.

Terrorism:
TRIA-compliant terrorism exclusions/inclusions on commercial
policies. Personal policies typically include limited
terrorism coverage.

Punitive damages:
Insurability varies by state — some states permit insurance
of punitive damages, some prohibit it, some permit only
vicarious punitives. Exclusions often track state law.


ENDORSEMENT EXCLUSIONS
=======================

In addition to base-form exclusions, exclusions can be added
by endorsement during underwriting. Common endorsement-added
exclusions:

- Total Pollution Exclusion (eliminates the carve-backs in
  the base pollution exclusion)
- Asbestos / lead / mold exclusions when the base form
  doesn't already have them
- Designated Operations Exclusion (excludes specific types of
  operations the insured engages in)
- Designated Premises Exclusion (excludes losses at specific
  named locations)
- Communicable Disease Exclusion
- Cyber Exclusion (on legacy policies that didn't have one)
- Specific named risk exclusions (a particular product line
  the underwriter declined)

Endorsement exclusions take precedence over the base form
because they're the more specific document. If an endorsement
exclusion conflicts with base-form coverage, the endorsement
controls.


HOW COURTS READ EXCLUSIONS
============================

Insurance contracts are construed in favor of coverage:

- Ambiguity is read against the carrier (contra proferentem
  — interpreted against the drafter)
- Exclusions are narrowly construed; coverage grants
  broadly construed
- The reasonable expectations of the insured matter,
  especially when the policy language is technical
- The carrier bears the burden of proving an exclusion
  applies

These doctrines push back against overly broad exclusion
language. Carriers have responded with more specific
exclusion drafting over time — older, broader exclusion
language has often been narrowed or replaced.


READING AN EXCLUSION IN PRACTICE
=================================

When evaluating whether an exclusion applies to a claim:

1. Read the affirmative coverage grant — is the claim within
   it in principle?
2. Read the exclusion as a whole — what category does it
   remove?
3. Identify carve-backs / exceptions — what restored
   coverage might apply?
4. Map the actual facts onto the exclusion language — does
   the exclusion clearly apply, or is there ambiguity?
5. Check for endorsements that may modify or override the
   base exclusion
6. Apply state-law construction principles — narrow reading
   of exclusions, ambiguity against the carrier

Many coverage disputes turn on which exclusion's specific
language matches the loss facts, not on broad principles. A
well-drafted exclusion is one whose words clearly fit the
exposures the carrier intended to exclude — and the litigation
record is full of cases where the words didn't fit cleanly.


PART TWO — CONDITIONS
======================


WHAT A CONDITION IS
====================

A condition is a provision specifying what the insured (and
sometimes the carrier) must DO for coverage to apply or
continue. Conditions are duties, procedures, and rules — they
don't grant or remove coverage directly, but failure to
comply can extinguish coverage for a claim.

Conditions vs exclusions:
- Exclusions remove a category of loss from coverage
- Conditions impose duties on the insured related to the
  claim or policy
- A loss may be within coverage and outside any exclusion,
  yet still denied because the insured breached a condition


COMMON CONDITIONS
==================

Prompt / timely notice of loss:
The insured must notify the carrier promptly after a loss.
Some policies specify a timeframe (within 30 days, within 60
days); most use "as soon as practicable" or "promptly."

Why it matters:
- Allows the carrier to investigate while evidence is fresh
- Allows control over claim handling and defense decisions
- Allows reserve setting and reinsurance reporting

Consequences of breach:
Vary by state. Some states require the carrier to show
PREJUDICE from late notice before denying — late notice
alone isn't enough if the carrier wasn't actually harmed.
Other states treat late notice as voiding coverage per se.
The trend has been toward requiring prejudice.

Duty to cooperate:
The insured must cooperate with the carrier's investigation
— providing statements, attending depositions, producing
documents, attending examinations under oath if demanded.

For third-party claims, cooperation extends to defense —
the insured must assist defense counsel, not undermine the
defense (e.g. by giving statements adverse to the carrier's
defense position).

Consequences of breach:
Material non-cooperation can void coverage. Courts generally
require the carrier to show the non-cooperation was
substantial and prejudicial.

Proof of Loss:
For property claims, the insured must submit a sworn
statement documenting the loss — typically within a stated
period (often 60 days from carrier's request). Proof of loss
formally establishes what the insured is claiming and
triggers carrier deadlines to accept, reject, or request
more information.

No voluntary payments / no admission of liability:
The insured cannot make payments, assume obligations, or
admit liability without the carrier's consent. Protects the
carrier from the insured undermining defense or settling
prematurely. Carve-backs typically permit emergency first
aid and protection-of-property expenses.

Subrogation / transfer of rights of recovery:
The insured assigns to the carrier any right to recover from
third parties for the loss the carrier paid. The insured
must cooperate with subrogation pursuit and must not waive
or impair subrogation rights without consent.

Waiver of subrogation: the insured may waive subrogation
against specific parties IF done in writing BEFORE the loss
(a written waiver of subrogation endorsement, or contractual
language pre-loss). Waiving subrogation post-loss without
carrier consent breaches the condition.

Other Insurance:
Specifies how the policy responds when other insurance also
applies to the same loss. Common structures:

- Primary: this policy responds first up to its limit
- Excess: this policy responds only after other insurance
  is exhausted
- Pro rata: this policy and other insurance share
  proportionally
- Escape: this policy provides no coverage if other
  insurance applies (older, disfavored)

When two policies both claim to be excess to each other,
courts often impose pro rata sharing as a fallback.

Cancellation and non-renewal:
Specifies the procedures for cancelling or non-renewing the
policy — notice timeframes, permissible reasons,
return-premium calculation. Heavily regulated by state law
to protect consumers and notice requirements (typically 10
days for non-payment, 30-45 days for other reasons).

Audit (commercial):
For policies rated on auditable exposure bases (workers comp
payroll, GL receipts/payroll, commercial auto fleets), the
carrier has the right to audit the insured's records at year-
end. Premium is adjusted up or down based on actual exposure.
Failure to cooperate with audit can result in policy
cancellation and presumed exposure billing.

Examination Under Oath (EUO):
The carrier may demand the insured submit to a sworn formal
examination on claim facts. Common in suspected fraud
investigations. Refusing to attend an EUO can breach the
cooperation condition and void coverage for the claim.

Action against the company / legal action clause:
Sets the procedural prerequisites for the insured to sue the
carrier — typically requires full compliance with policy
conditions, exhaustion of internal claim procedures, and
suit filed within a specified period (often two years).

Concealment, misrepresentation, or fraud:
Voids the policy (in some states, voids it ab initio — from
the beginning) if the insured misrepresents material facts or
conceals information from the carrier, whether at
application, during the policy period, or in claims. The
standard is usually MATERIALITY — the misrepresentation must
have affected the carrier's underwriting or claim decision.

Severability of conditions:
Some conditions are severable — one insured's breach doesn't
void coverage for innocent co-insureds. Important on policies
with multiple insureds (corporate policies, family policies).
Read the specific severability language; it varies.

Bankruptcy of the insured:
Bankruptcy or insolvency of the insured doesn't relieve the
carrier of its obligations. The carrier continues to be
liable for covered claims even when the insured can't pay
deductibles or self-insured retentions (though the carrier
may take a position against the bankrupt estate for
unrecovered SIRs).

Inspection rights:
The carrier has the right to inspect insured property and
operations. Used for underwriting (initial inspection),
periodic monitoring, and post-loss investigation. Refusing
inspection rights breaches the condition.

Premium audit (commercial):
Already noted above — annual audit on auditable lines.

Choice of law / forum selection:
Specifies what state's law governs disputes and where suit
must be filed. Enforcement varies by state — some
jurisdictions decline to enforce forum-selection clauses
that disadvantage insureds.


CONDITIONS vs WARRANTIES
=========================

A related concept that's now largely historical:

Warranty:
In older insurance law, a "warranty" was a strict statement
of fact by the insured — and any breach, regardless of
materiality, voided the policy. Modern statute has largely
eliminated strict warranty doctrine in favor of materiality
standards: a misstatement voids coverage only if it was
material to the carrier's underwriting or claim decision.

Representation:
A statement the insured makes during application. Modern law
treats most application statements as representations subject
to materiality, not warranties subject to strict compliance.

Conditions:
Continuing duties during the policy period — distinct from
warranties (one-time statements) and representations
(application statements).


CONSEQUENCES OF CONDITION BREACH
=================================

The consequences of breaching a condition depend on:

The nature of the condition:
- Conditions precedent to coverage: must be performed for
  coverage to attach. Failure means no coverage for that
  claim.
- Conditions subsequent: ongoing obligations whose breach may
  void coverage or trigger other remedies.

State law on prejudice:
Many states require the carrier to show actual prejudice
before a condition breach voids coverage — the carrier must
prove the breach actually harmed its investigation, defense,
or position. The "no harm, no foul" approach has gained
ground over strict per-se voiding.

The specific policy language:
Some conditions are absolute by their terms ("no coverage
shall be afforded under this policy unless..."). Others are
softer and require the carrier to show prejudice or
materiality.

Severability:
On policies with multiple insureds, severability provisions
may preserve coverage for innocent co-insureds even when one
insured breaches a condition.


READING CONDITIONS IN PRACTICE
================================

When evaluating whether a condition has been breached:

1. Identify the specific condition at issue
2. Determine what the condition requires (timeline, action,
   notice, etc.)
3. Compare the insured's actual conduct to the requirement
4. Determine whether the policy makes the condition
   absolute or subject to prejudice / materiality
5. Apply state law on prejudice requirements
6. Check severability — does breach by one insured affect
   innocent co-insureds?

Coverage denials based on condition breach are common but
often contestable. Insureds and their counsel should not
assume that a denial based on "late notice" or "failure to
cooperate" is the end of the matter — the carrier may need
to demonstrate prejudice that didn't actually occur.


HOW EXCLUSIONS AND CONDITIONS INTERACT WITH ENDORSEMENTS
==========================================================

Endorsements can modify exclusions and conditions just as
they modify coverage grants:

- Endorsement adds an exclusion not in the base form
  (Designated Operations, Total Pollution, etc.)
- Endorsement REMOVES or NARROWS a base-form exclusion
  (Water Backup endorsement narrows the water exclusion;
  Ordinance or Law endorsement narrows the code-upgrade
  exclusion)
- Endorsement modifies a condition (adds an additional
  insured with different notice obligations; extends the
  reporting period on a claims-made policy)
- Endorsement creates a separate condition (a specific
  warranty endorsement requiring particular safety
  equipment, for example)

Order of precedence: endorsements take precedence over base
form. When endorsement language conflicts with base form
language, the endorsement controls. When endorsements
conflict with each other, courts typically read them
together if possible, with ambiguities favoring coverage.


COVERAGE ANALYSIS — PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER
=============================================

A complete coverage analysis for a claim requires:

1. Identify the relevant declarations (named insured, policy
   period, limits, attached forms)
2. Read the insuring agreement — what does the policy
   affirmatively cover?
3. Read the definitions — what do the key terms mean?
4. Check exclusions — does any exclusion remove this loss
   from coverage?
5. Check carve-backs to exclusions — does any exception
   restore coverage?
6. Read all endorsements in order — do any add, remove, or
   modify coverage, exclusions, or conditions?
7. Check conditions — has the insured complied with all
   applicable duties?
8. Apply state-law construction principles to any ambiguity
9. Document the analysis with specific policy language
   citations

The order matters because conclusions at each step affect
later steps — an exclusion that initially seems to apply may
be narrowed by an endorsement carve-back, or a condition
breach that appears to void coverage may not actually do so
under state prejudice law.


KEY TAKEAWAYS
=============

- Exclusions remove specific categories from the affirmative
  coverage grant; the carrier bears the burden of proving
  they apply
- Common exclusions reflect line-of-business boundaries,
  separately insured exposures, catastrophic risks, public
  policy limits, account underwriting decisions, and
  fortuity requirements
- Carve-backs (exceptions to exclusions) often matter as
  much as the exclusions themselves
- Conditions impose duties on the insured — prompt notice,
  cooperation, proof of loss, no voluntary payments,
  subrogation cooperation, and others
- Many states require prejudice before condition breach voids
  coverage; older per-se voiding has lost ground
- Endorsements can modify both exclusions and conditions and
  take precedence over the base form
- A complete coverage analysis works through declarations,
  insuring agreement, definitions, exclusions and carve-
  backs, endorsements, and conditions in order
