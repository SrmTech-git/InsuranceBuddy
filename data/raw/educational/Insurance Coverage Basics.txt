Insurance Coverage Basics: Foundational Coverage Types
======================================================

This document explains the foundational coverage building blocks that
make up most personal and commercial insurance policies. These are the
concepts that show up everywhere in policy language, applications,
declarations pages, and certificates — the "what does X mean?"
reference for the most common abbreviations and coverage terms.

This is a general industry-level overview. Specific policy language,
limits, and exclusions vary by carrier, state, and form — always
defer to the actual policy and applicable statutes for binding
coverage determinations.


LIABILITY FOUNDATIONS
=====================

Bodily Injury (BI)
------------------
Bodily Injury coverage pays for physical injury, sickness, or disease
— including death — that an insured causes to others. It typically
covers medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering, and other
damages a claimant can recover for personal harm.

In an auto policy, BI is the part of liability that responds when the
insured driver is at fault for an accident that injures someone else.
In a general liability policy, it covers injuries to third parties
arising from the insured's premises, operations, or products.

BI limits are usually expressed as two numbers: per-person and
per-accident (sometimes called per-occurrence). For example,
"100/300" means $100,000 per person and $300,000 per accident. The
per-person limit caps any one claimant's recovery; the per-accident
limit caps total recovery across all injured parties from a single
event.

BI does NOT cover injuries to the insured themselves — those go to
Medical Payments, PIP, health insurance, or workers compensation
depending on context.


Property Damage (PD)
--------------------
Property Damage liability covers damage the insured causes to other
people's property — buildings, vehicles, contents, fences, anything
that isn't a person's body. PD is the companion to BI in liability
coverage; together they form the bulk of standard liability protection.

Auto PD typically pays to repair or replace another driver's vehicle,
road signs the insured hits, fences, mailboxes, and similar property.
Commercial GL PD covers damage to a customer's property at the
insured's premises, damage caused by the insured's products after they
leave, or damage during operations on someone else's property.

PD limits are usually expressed as a single per-accident number —
"25" in "100/300/25" means $25,000 of property damage coverage per
accident.


Combined Single Limit (CSL)
---------------------------
A Combined Single Limit replaces the BI per-person, BI per-accident,
and PD limits with one shared bucket. For example, a $1,000,000 CSL
means up to $1M total can be paid for any combination of BI and PD
from one accident — no internal sublimits.

CSL is more flexible than split limits because the full limit can
apply to a single person if needed, but it usually costs more for
equivalent overall protection.


Medical Payments (MP / Med Pay)
-------------------------------
Medical Payments is no-fault coverage that pays medical bills for the
insured, passengers (in an auto policy), or guests on the premises (in
a homeowners policy), regardless of who was at fault. It's typically a
smaller limit ($1,000 to $10,000 is common) and pays quickly without
litigation.

MP fills gaps that BI doesn't address. If a passenger is injured in
the insured's car, BI might not respond if no one is legally liable,
but MP pays the medical bills anyway. In a homeowners policy, MP pays
for guests who are injured on the property.


Premises Liability
------------------
Premises liability is the part of general liability covering injuries
that happen on, or as a result of, the insured's physical premises.
Slips and falls, trips, falling objects, inadequate maintenance —
these are premises claims. Premises liability is usually the bulk of a
small business's GL exposure.


Products and Completed Operations
---------------------------------
This part of GL covers injuries or damage caused by the insured's
products after they leave the insured's possession, or by the
insured's work after the job is complete. A contractor whose installed
deck collapses six months later, a manufacturer whose product injures
a consumer, a restaurant whose food causes illness — these are
products / completed operations claims.

This coverage typically has its own aggregate limit separate from the
general aggregate.


Personal and Advertising Injury
-------------------------------
This part of GL covers a specific list of "personal" wrongs — false
arrest, malicious prosecution, wrongful eviction, defamation (libel
and slander), violation of right of privacy — and "advertising"
wrongs like copyright infringement in advertising or use of another's
advertising idea.

Despite the name, "personal injury" here doesn't mean bodily injury
(the standard auto/GL definition is different). It's a separate
category of intentional-tort-like exposures.


General Liability (GL)
----------------------
General Liability is the broad category of coverage protecting a
business from third-party claims for bodily injury, property damage,
and personal/advertising injury arising out of premises, operations,
products, and completed operations. It does NOT cover injuries to the
insured's own employees (workers compensation territory), professional
errors (E&O / professional liability), or auto-related liability
(commercial auto).

Most commercial businesses carry GL as a baseline. The standard ISO
Commercial General Liability form (CGL) bundles premises, operations,
products / completed operations, personal & advertising injury, and
medical payments under one policy.


AUTO-SPECIFIC COVERAGES
=======================

Uninsured Motorist (UM)
-----------------------
Uninsured Motorist coverage protects the insured when they're injured
by a driver who has no liability insurance. Since the at-fault driver
can't pay (no insurance), UM steps in and pays the insured for their
bodily injury — essentially reaching into the insured's own policy as
a substitute for the missing liable party's coverage.

UM is required in some states and optional in others. Where optional,
it's typically offered at limits matching the policy's BI limits
unless the insured rejects it in writing.


Underinsured Motorist (UIM)
---------------------------
Underinsured Motorist covers the gap when an at-fault driver has SOME
insurance but not enough. If the insured's damages exceed the at-fault
driver's limits, UIM picks up the difference up to the UIM limit. UM
and UIM are commonly bundled as "UM/UIM" since the structure and
rationale are nearly identical.


Personal Injury Protection (PIP)
--------------------------------
PIP is no-fault medical and economic loss coverage on auto policies,
primarily found in no-fault states (Florida, Michigan, New York, New
Jersey, and others). It pays the insured's medical bills, lost wages,
and sometimes essential services costs after an accident — regardless
of who was at fault — up to the PIP limit.

PIP requirements and structure vary heavily by state. Some states
make PIP mandatory; others make it optional with rejection
requirements; a few don't have PIP at all.


Collision (Coll)
----------------
Collision pays for damage to the insured's own vehicle from impact —
hitting another vehicle, object, or rolling over — regardless of
fault. Subject to a deductible.


Comprehensive / Other Than Collision (OTC)
------------------------------------------
Comprehensive (sometimes called "Other Than Collision") covers damage
to the insured's vehicle from causes other than collision: fire,
theft, vandalism, hail, flood, hitting an animal, falling objects.
Subject to a deductible, often lower than collision.

Together, Collision and Comprehensive are the "physical damage"
portion of an auto policy — they protect the vehicle itself rather
than third parties.


LIMIT STRUCTURES
================

Split Limits
------------
Split limits express liability coverage as three numbers: BI per
person / BI per accident / PD per accident. Example: 25/50/25 means
$25,000 per person BI, $50,000 per accident BI, $25,000 PD per
accident. Split limits are common on personal auto and small
commercial auto.


Aggregate Limits
----------------
An aggregate limit caps the total amount the policy will pay during a
policy period (usually 12 months) regardless of how many claims
occur. GL policies typically have a general aggregate (for premises
and operations) and a separate products / completed operations
aggregate. Once the aggregate is exhausted, the policy doesn't pay
further claims that period — even if individual per-occurrence limits
remain.


Per-Occurrence Limits
---------------------
The most a policy will pay for any one occurrence (event triggering
coverage). Each new occurrence has its own per-occurrence limit, but
each one also draws from the aggregate.


Deductibles, Self-Insured Retentions (SIR), and Coinsurance
-----------------------------------------------------------
Deductible: the insured pays the first dollar amount of a covered
loss before the policy responds. The carrier handles defense and
pays losses, then bills the insured for the deductible.

Self-Insured Retention (SIR): similar to a deductible, but the
insured handles the loss (including defense) up to the retention
amount; the carrier doesn't get involved until the SIR is exhausted.

Coinsurance: a property insurance concept — if the insured doesn't
carry coverage at the percentage of value required (e.g. 80% or 90%),
partial losses get penalized. A 90% coinsurance clause says the
insured must carry coverage at 90% of replacement cost; carrying less
means losses are paid at a reduced ratio.
