Professional Liability (Errors and Omissions) Insurance
========================================================

This document explains what Professional Liability / Errors and
Omissions (E&O) insurance is, what it covers, how it differs
structurally from general liability, the common claims scenarios,
and the variations across professions.


WHAT PROFESSIONAL LIABILITY COVERS
===================================

Professional Liability — also called Errors and Omissions (E&O)
— covers a professional's legal liability for financial harm
caused to a client by negligent acts, errors, or omissions in
the performance of professional services.

The core risk: a client hires a professional, the professional
makes a mistake (or fails to do something they should have),
the client suffers financial loss as a result, and the client
sues. General liability won't respond because GL excludes
"professional services" — that's E&O's job.

Key distinction from CGL:
- CGL covers bodily injury and property damage
- E&O covers economic loss / financial damage from professional
  mistakes
- A doctor leaving a sponge in a patient is malpractice (a form
  of E&O); a wet floor in the waiting room is GL
- An accountant filing taxes incorrectly causes financial harm
  with no bodily injury or property damage — pure E&O

What "professional services" means:
The definition is in the policy, often listed by activity or
profession. Typical coverage attaches to advice, recommendations,
calculations, designs, opinions, certifications, or any service
requiring specialized training or expertise.


CLAIMS-MADE vs OCCURRENCE — A CRITICAL DISTINCTION
===================================================

Most professional liability policies are written on a CLAIMS-
MADE basis, not occurrence. This is one of the most important
structural differences between E&O and most other liability
lines (which are usually occurrence).

Occurrence policy:
Responds to losses that OCCUR during the policy period,
regardless of when the claim is made. A 2020 occurrence policy
covers a 2020 incident even if the claim doesn't come in until
2026. This is how CGL typically works.

Claims-made policy:
Responds to CLAIMS made and reported during the policy period,
regardless of when the underlying incident occurred (subject to
retroactive date — see below). A claims-made policy bound in
2026 covers a 2020 incident only if (a) the claim is made and
reported in 2026, and (b) the retroactive date is 2020 or
earlier.

Why E&O is claims-made:
Professional liability losses often surface long after the
service was performed (an accounting error discovered three
years later in audit; a design defect that doesn't manifest
until years after construction). Claims-made structure lets
the carrier price for the claims actually reported in the
year, rather than waiting decades to know what a policy year
will ultimately cost. Occurrence pricing for E&O would be
nearly impossible to predict.

Retroactive date:
The "look-back" boundary. The policy covers acts/errors that
occurred on or after the retroactive date. An act/error before
the retro date is not covered, even if the claim comes in
during the current policy period.

When you change carriers or rewrite an E&O policy, preserving
the original retroactive date is critical. A new policy with a
new retroactive date (the renewal date) creates a coverage
gap — work done before the new retro date is no longer
covered by the new policy, and the old policy is no longer in
force to respond.

Extended Reporting Period (ERP) / Tail coverage:
When a claims-made policy terminates (carrier change,
retirement, business sale), claims that report AFTER
termination for acts during the policy period would otherwise
have no coverage. ERP / tail coverage extends the reporting
window beyond termination — common options are 1, 3, 5, or
unlimited years. Critical for retiring professionals or
businesses winding down operations.

Prior Acts / Nose coverage:
The reverse situation — when starting a new claims-made policy
after a gap, prior acts coverage agrees to cover work done
before the new policy started (subject to a retroactive date).
Used to bridge from a prior policy or to provide coverage when
prior-policy tail isn't purchased.


COVERAGE PARTS
==============

Most professional liability policies include:

Liability coverage:
Pays damages the insured is legally obligated to pay because of
a covered wrongful act in the rendering of professional
services. Includes covered judgments, settlements, and related
costs.

Defense:
Attorney fees, court costs, expert witness fees. Depending on
the policy:
- Defense costs IN ADDITION to the limit (broader — more
  common in higher-end policies)
- Defense costs ERODE the limit (less broad — defense reduces
  what's available to pay damages, common in lower-cost
  policies)

The "eroding limits" structure can be a problem on long-
running, expensive cases — defense costs can consume the
limit before any damages are even decided.

Disciplinary proceedings:
Some E&O policies cover defense costs for licensing board /
disciplinary proceedings even when no monetary claim is made.
Important for licensed professionals where a board complaint
can damage reputation and license status regardless of
whether the client sues.

Subpoena response:
Costs of responding to subpoenas about the insured's
professional services in cases where the insured isn't a party
— increasingly common as a coverage feature.


COMMON E&O EXCLUSIONS
======================

Standard exclusions across most E&O forms:

- Bodily injury and property damage (these belong on CGL —
  except in medical malpractice, which is itself a specialty
  E&O variant that DOES cover BI)
- Intentional / dishonest / fraudulent acts (with severability
  — typically only the perpetrator is excluded, innocent
  partners can still have defense)
- Criminal acts
- Insured vs insured (one partner suing another in the same
  firm; carve-backs for whistleblower and certain employment
  claims)
- Prior known acts (claims the insured was aware of before
  policy inception)
- Contractual liability beyond what the law would impose
  (liability the insured assumed in a contract above and
  beyond common-law duty)
- Patent infringement (often excluded; may be covered with
  endorsement on tech/IP-focused E&O)
- Pollution (separate environmental coverage)
- War and nuclear
- Specific professional services not declared on the
  application


PROFESSIONS COMMONLY E&O-INSURED
================================

Different professions have different coverage forms, limit
norms, and pricing patterns:

Medical professionals (Medical Malpractice):
Doctors, nurses, dentists, chiropractors, psychotherapists.
Often written on specialty malpractice forms; very state-
specific due to varying tort reform regimes (caps on damages,
collateral source rules, joint and several liability rules).
Limits typically $1M/$3M (per claim / aggregate) for routine
practice, much higher for high-risk specialties.

Legal professionals (Lawyers Professional Liability / LPL):
Attorneys and law firms. Bar association-sponsored programs
common in some states; commercial market also active. Limits
scale with practice size and area (M&A and securities firms
carry far higher limits than family law solo practitioners).

Architects and Engineers (A&E):
Design professionals. Long claims tails (errors may not
manifest until construction completes years later). Many
contracts require A&E professionals to carry specific limits
and waiver of subrogation. Coverage often integrated with
project-specific Professional Liability for large
construction projects.

Accountants:
CPAs, bookkeepers, tax preparers. Coverage often includes
defense for IRS proceedings and state tax authority
proceedings.

Insurance Agents and Brokers:
E&O for the act of placing insurance — failing to recommend
adequate coverage, misrepresenting policy terms, failure to
report a claim timely. Required by most carriers as a
condition of agent appointment.

Real Estate Agents and Brokers:
Liability for disclosure failures, misrepresentation of
property condition, failure to advise on inspections.

Financial Advisors:
Investment advisors, broker-dealers, financial planners.
Coverage for suitability claims, misrepresentation, failure
to follow client instructions. Often paired with specialized
fiduciary coverage.

Consultants:
Management consultants, IT consultants, marketing
consultants. Broad "miscellaneous professional liability"
forms accommodate the variety.

Technology Professionals (Tech E&O):
Software developers, IT services, SaaS providers, managed
service providers. Modern Tech E&O often integrates with
Cyber coverage — a single integrated form covering both
professional errors AND data breach exposure. (See Cyber
Liability Overview for the cyber side.)

Miscellaneous Professional Liability:
For professions not covered by a dedicated form — translators,
mediators, expert witnesses, educational consultants, etc.


COMMON CLAIMS SCENARIOS
========================

Accountants:
- Tax filing error causes penalty / interest assessment
- Audit fails to detect embezzlement or fraud
- Bookkeeping error causes business decisions made on bad data

Lawyers:
- Statute of limitations missed
- Conflict of interest not disclosed
- Inadequate due diligence on a transaction
- Negligent drafting of contract or estate document

Architects / Engineers:
- Design defect discovered after construction
- Failure to specify code-compliant materials
- Inadequate site analysis leading to foundation problems
- Specification error causing cost overrun

Insurance Agents:
- Failure to procure requested coverage
- Failure to advise client of available coverage
- Failure to report claim to carrier within policy timeframe
- Misrepresentation of policy terms

Real Estate:
- Failure to disclose known property defect
- Misrepresentation of square footage or zoning
- Failure to recommend inspection

Financial Advisors:
- Unsuitable investment recommendation
- Unauthorized trading
- Failure to execute client instructions
- Inadequate disclosure of fees or risks


LIMITS AND DEDUCTIBLES
=======================

Per claim / aggregate structure:
Most E&O policies have a per-claim limit and a separate
aggregate limit (e.g. $1M per claim / $3M aggregate). The
per-claim limit caps what's available for any single claim;
the aggregate caps total payouts across all claims in the
policy period.

Defense in or outside limits:
As above, defense costs may reduce or be in addition to the
limit. Always specified in declarations.

Deductible / SIR:
Most professional liability has a per-claim deductible — the
insured pays the first portion (e.g. $5K, $25K, $100K)
including defense costs that fall within the deductible.
Larger firms with higher deductibles often have their own
in-house counsel handling minor matters that resolve within
the deductible.

Some policies use a Self-Insured Retention (SIR) instead of
a deductible — the difference being that the SIR is paid
BEFORE the carrier's defense duty triggers; with a
deductible, the carrier defends from day one and seeks
reimbursement up to the deductible.


WHEN E&O ISN'T ENOUGH
======================

Common gaps an insured may need to address separately:

- Cyber breach / privacy liability — Cyber policy (sometimes
  integrated with Tech E&O)
- Employment practices (wrongful termination, discrimination)
  — EPLI
- Director/officer governance liability — D&O
- Crime / employee dishonesty — Crime policy or Fidelity bond
- Bodily injury at the office — CGL
- Bodily injury to employees — Workers Comp
- Auto liability — Commercial Auto


REGULATORY AND CONTRACTUAL REQUIREMENTS
========================================

Many professions have E&O requirements imposed externally:

- State bar associations may require minimum LPL coverage for
  attorneys in private practice
- Many state real estate commissions require minimum E&O for
  licensure
- Architecture and engineering boards in some states require
  E&O for licensure
- Most carriers require agents to carry minimum E&O as a
  condition of appointment
- Client contracts — especially in tech, consulting, and
  construction — increasingly specify minimum E&O limits,
  retroactive date provisions, and waiver of subrogation
- Government contracts often require specific E&O limits and
  endorsement of the government entity as additional insured


KEY TAKEAWAYS
=============

- E&O covers financial harm from professional mistakes;
  separate and distinct from CGL
- Claims-made structure means timing of CLAIM matters more
  than timing of underlying ACT
- Retroactive date and tail coverage are the structural
  elements that prevent coverage gaps on carrier changes or
  business termination
- Profession-specific forms vary substantially — what
  "professional services" means differs by profession
- Defense-in-limits vs defense-outside-limits is a major
  coverage-quality differentiator
- Common adjacent coverages (Cyber, EPLI, D&O) fill gaps E&O
  doesn't address
