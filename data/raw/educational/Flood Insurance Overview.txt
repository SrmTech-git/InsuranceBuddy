Flood Insurance: NFIP and Private Market
=========================================

This document explains how flood insurance works in the United
States — why it's separate from homeowners, how the National Flood
Insurance Program (NFIP) is structured, the standard SFIP forms,
flood zones, the private flood market, and the mandatory purchase
requirement.


WHY FLOOD IS A SEPARATE POLICY
===============================

Flood is excluded from virtually every standard homeowners,
dwelling, and commercial property policy. Reasons:

- Adverse selection: only people in flood-prone areas would buy,
  making it actuarially difficult to price
- Catastrophic concentration: a single flood event can cause
  widespread correlated losses, hard for a private carrier to
  absorb
- Historical: after the 1965 Betsy / 1968 floods, the private
  market largely withdrew from flood coverage

The federal government stepped in via the National Flood
Insurance Program (NFIP), created by the National Flood
Insurance Act of 1968. NFIP made flood insurance broadly
available where private carriers wouldn't write it.


WHAT FLOOD MEANS UNDER NFIP
============================

NFIP defines "flood" specifically — and the definition matters
because not all water damage is flood damage. A flood under SFIP
is:

"A general and temporary condition of partial or complete
inundation of two or more acres of normally dry land area or of
two or more properties (at least one of which is the policyholder's
property) from:
- Overflow of inland or tidal waters
- Unusual and rapid accumulation or runoff of surface waters
  from any source
- Mudflow
- Collapse or subsidence of land along the shore of a lake or
  similar body of water as a result of erosion or undermining
  caused by waves or currents of water exceeding anticipated
  cyclical levels"

What this means in practice:
- A burst pipe inside the home is NOT flood — it's covered (or
  excluded) under the homeowners policy.
- A sewer backup is NOT flood — it's covered under the HO water
  backup endorsement (HO 04 95) if added; otherwise excluded.
- Surface water entering from outside — heavy rain causing yard
  runoff into the basement — IS flood under SFIP.
- Storm surge from a hurricane IS flood (a recurring source of
  litigation after coastal hurricanes — wind damage covered by
  HO/commercial, water damage covered only by flood).


HOW NFIP IS STRUCTURED
======================

Administering agency:
FEMA (Federal Emergency Management Agency) administers NFIP. FEMA
sets rates, defines maps, and underwrites the program.

Distribution:
NFIP policies are written through two channels:
- Direct from NFIP via the NFIP Direct Servicing Agent
- Through "Write Your Own" (WYO) carriers — private carriers that
  issue NFIP policies under their own brand, administer them, and
  handle claims, but the federal government bears the risk

The insured experiences NFIP through WYO carriers — but the
contract is the federally defined SFIP, the rates are federally
set, and the claim is federally funded.

Community participation:
NFIP coverage is only available in communities that participate
in the program by adopting floodplain management standards. Most
US communities participate.


SFIP — STANDARD FLOOD INSURANCE POLICY
=======================================

Three SFIP forms:

Dwelling Form:
For 1-4 family residential structures the insured owns and
occupies (or rents out). Most homeowners flood policies.

General Property Form:
For non-residential structures, residential structures with 5+
units, and certain other risks. Commercial flood policies.

Residential Condominium Building Association Policy (RCBAP):
Written to the condo association for the entire residential
condo building (5+ units). Unit owners may then carry separate
SFIPs for their contents and improvements.


NFIP COVERAGE LIMITS
====================

NFIP has STATUTORY MAXIMUM LIMITS — caps Congress sets, not
underwriting choices:

Dwelling Form (1-4 family):
- $250,000 building coverage
- $100,000 contents coverage

General Property Form (non-residential):
- $500,000 building coverage
- $500,000 contents coverage

RCBAP (condo association):
- $250,000 × number of units, up to the replacement cost of the
  building

Insureds whose exposure exceeds NFIP caps need supplemental
"excess flood" coverage from the private market.


FLOOD ZONES AND MAPS
====================

FEMA publishes Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRMs) that classify
every property's flood risk. Common zone designations:

Zone X (or Zone C / Zone B on older maps):
Minimal flood hazard — areas outside the 500-year floodplain.
Flood insurance is OPTIONAL and typically inexpensive.

Zone X (shaded):
Moderate flood hazard — between the 100-year and 500-year
floodplains. Coverage optional, modestly priced.

Zone A / AE / AH / AO / AR:
Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA) — within the 100-year
floodplain (1% annual chance of flooding). Coverage is REQUIRED
for federally-backed mortgages. Higher premium.

Zone V / VE:
Coastal SFHA — wave action exposure on top of inundation. Highest
premium tier.

Zone D:
Undetermined risk.

The 100-year flood is the federal regulatory boundary — properties
inside it must carry flood insurance if they have a federally
backed mortgage. "100-year" doesn't mean the flood happens once
a century — it means a 1% chance EACH YEAR, which over a 30-year
mortgage gives roughly a 26% cumulative chance.


MANDATORY PURCHASE REQUIREMENT
===============================

The Flood Disaster Protection Act of 1973 and subsequent
legislation require federally-regulated lenders to demand flood
insurance on properties in SFHAs (Zone A or V). Specifically:

- If the property is in an SFHA AND
- The mortgage is from a federally-regulated lender OR backed by
  a federal agency (FHA, VA, USDA, Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac)
- Then flood insurance covering at least the mortgage balance or
  the maximum NFIP limit (whichever is less) must be maintained
  for the life of the loan

Failure to maintain triggers force-placed flood insurance by the
lender — much more expensive than NFIP-direct.

Lenders verify flood-zone determinations at closing using a Flood
Hazard Determination Form. Mid-mortgage map changes can trigger
new requirements.


WAITING PERIOD
==============

NFIP policies have a 30-DAY WAITING PERIOD before coverage
attaches. Exceptions:

- Coverage tied to a mortgage closing (effective at closing)
- Coverage tied to a map change putting the property in a new
  SFHA (1-day waiting period if purchased within 13 months of
  the map change)
- Renewals (no waiting period)

The waiting period prevents buying flood insurance the day a
storm is forecast. It also catches insureds who delayed —
hurricane forecasts that are 4 weeks out are too close to closing
the gap.


RISK RATING 2.0
================

In October 2021, FEMA shifted NFIP from zone-based pricing to
"Risk Rating 2.0" — a property-specific pricing model that
considers:
- Distance to the nearest flooding source
- Elevation
- Construction type and foundation
- Cost to rebuild
- Frequency of flooding at the specific property

The shift was designed to make pricing more actuarially accurate.
Premiums for previously under-priced properties rose; premiums for
properties further from flooding fell. Premium increases are
phased in (capped annual increase of 18%) to avoid sticker shock.


INCREASED COST OF COMPLIANCE (ICC)
===================================

SFIPs include up to $30,000 of Increased Cost of Compliance
coverage. If a property is "substantially damaged" (damage at
least 50% of pre-loss market value) by a flood AND local
regulations require it to be rebuilt to current floodplain
standards (elevation, demolition, relocation), ICC pays toward
those code-required improvements.

ICC is critical in repetitive-loss properties — rebuilding to
current code may be expensive, and without ICC the homeowner
absorbs the cost.


PRIVATE FLOOD MARKET
====================

Since the late 2010s, a private flood insurance market has
developed offering:

- Excess flood — fills the gap above NFIP's statutory caps
- Primary flood — competes directly with NFIP at higher limits,
  sometimes broader coverage (e.g. additional living expense,
  pool repair, replacement cost on contents)
- High-net-worth flood — packaged by Chubb, AIG Private Client,
  PURE for high-value homes

Private flood has grown rapidly in coastal Florida, the Gulf
states, and other high-exposure markets. Lenders increasingly
accept private flood policies as satisfying the mandatory
purchase requirement, though acceptance has historically been
uneven and was clarified by the 2019 Private Flood Insurance
Final Rule.


SFIP EXCLUSIONS
===============

NFIP SFIP excludes:
- Damage from water from a source inside the home (burst pipes,
  appliance leaks) — that's HO
- Additional living expense (NFIP DOES NOT cover loss of use —
  a major gap; private flood often does)
- Loss of business income (commercial flood likewise)
- Property in basements above limited contents categories (washer,
  dryer, freezer, water heater, fuel tank, items installed in
  their functioning location — but not personal property like
  furniture, electronics, or finished walls/ceilings)
- Mold/mildew not caused by flooding
- Land, lawn, trees, shrubs, fences
- Currency, precious metals, valuable papers
- Loss caused by sewer backup not directly attributable to a
  flood event
- Earth movement (covered separately by earthquake or excluded
  entirely)


WHEN FLOOD COVERAGE IS NOT ENOUGH
==================================

Common gaps:
- Property worth more than $250K building / $100K contents in a
  1-4 family — need excess flood
- Property needs loss-of-use coverage — base SFIP doesn't include
  ALE; private flood often does
- Basement-heavy losses — SFIP limits basement contents
  significantly; HO water backup might be a better fit for some
  causes
- Storm surge in a no-mandatory-purchase area — coverage is
  optional but storm surge claims are denied under HO


KEY TAKEAWAYS
=============

- Flood is excluded from HO and commercial property policies
- NFIP through FEMA is the primary US flood market, supplemented
  by private flood
- SFHA properties with federally backed mortgages must carry
  flood
- NFIP caps coverage at $250K/$100K for 1-4 family — excess
  flood fills gaps above that
- 30-day waiting period applies except at mortgage closing
- "Flood" has a specific NFIP definition — surface water from
  outside, not internal plumbing failures
- Risk Rating 2.0 is the current pricing methodology — property-
  specific, not just zone-based
