Cyber Liability Insurance
=========================

This document explains what cyber liability insurance is, the
distinctive first-party / third-party split, what kinds of
incidents trigger coverage, the typical exclusions, and how the
market has evolved as ransomware and breach costs have grown.


WHAT CYBER LIABILITY COVERS
============================

Cyber liability insurance covers losses arising from:
- Data breaches involving personally identifiable information,
  protected health information, or other sensitive data
- Ransomware and other cyber extortion events
- Network security failures
- Business interruption from a cyber incident
- Liability to third parties harmed by a security or privacy
  failure
- Regulatory investigations and fines tied to privacy /
  security incidents

Why standalone cyber exists:
Traditional property and liability policies were written
before digital exposure existed and contain explicit cyber
exclusions or simply don't fit the loss patterns. A
ransomware payment isn't bodily injury or property damage in
the CGL sense. A data breach isn't a tangible property loss
in the property-insurance sense. Cyber policies were
developed to address losses the legacy market wasn't
covering.


FIRST-PARTY vs THIRD-PARTY COVERAGE
=====================================

Cyber policies are split into two main coverage groups —
first-party (the insured's own losses) and third-party
(liability to others).

FIRST-PARTY COVERAGES:

Breach response / incident response:
The costs of responding to a confirmed or suspected breach:
- Forensic investigation (identifying scope and cause)
- Notification of affected individuals (often statutorily
  required)
- Credit monitoring and identity theft remediation services
  for affected individuals
- Public relations and crisis management
- Legal review of notification obligations and regulatory
  reporting

Breach response is typically the FIRST coverage triggered in
an incident and often consumes substantial dollars before
any third-party liability claim arises.

Business interruption (cyber BI):
Lost income and continuing expenses when a security incident
or system failure disrupts the insured's operations.
Distinct from property-policy BI, which typically requires
physical damage to trigger.

Common variations:
- "System failure" coverage: BI from accidental IT failure,
  not just malicious incidents
- "Dependent business interruption": BI from a cyber event
  at a key vendor (a cloud provider outage, a critical SaaS
  service failure)

Waiting period / time deductible:
Cyber BI typically has a time deductible — coverage attaches
only after a stated number of hours of outage (8, 12, 24
hours common). Brief outages don't trigger.

Cyber extortion / ransomware:
Coverage for ransom demands and the related response —
ransom payment itself (in policies that permit it), incident
response, negotiation services, decryption assistance,
restoration costs. The ransomware-coverage market has
tightened substantially as ransomware frequency has spiked.

Data restoration:
Costs to restore corrupted or destroyed data and software
from backups or by rebuilding. Doesn't cover business losses
during restoration — that's BI's job.

Funds transfer fraud / social engineering / cyber crime:
Coverage for funds lost when an employee is tricked into
transferring money to a fraudulent recipient (a fake CEO
email instructing a wire transfer, an invoice fraud
intercepting vendor payments). This coverage overlaps with
crime policies — important to coordinate.

Bricking coverage:
Covers cost of replacing hardware rendered unusable by a
malware attack (firmware destruction, infected IoT devices).
Less common but growing.

Reputational harm / loss of trust:
Income lost from customer flight after a publicized breach.
Often offered as a separate sub-coverage with its own
waiting period and proof requirements.

THIRD-PARTY COVERAGES:

Privacy liability:
Liability to individuals whose personal information was
exposed in a breach. Includes defense and indemnity for
class actions, individual suits, and regulatory enforcement.

Network security liability:
Liability to third parties harmed by a security failure on
the insured's network — for example, malware spreading
from the insured's network to a customer's network, or the
insured's compromised systems being used to attack others.

Regulatory defense and fines:
Costs of defending privacy/security regulatory investigations
(FTC, state AGs, HHS for HIPAA, EU regulators for GDPR,
California for CCPA/CPRA, etc.) AND, where insurable, the
fines and penalties themselves. Fine insurability varies by
jurisdiction — GDPR fines insurability is contested in some
EU member states.

Media liability / multimedia:
Liability for content the insured publishes — defamation,
copyright infringement, trademark infringement, invasion of
privacy. Important for content-heavy businesses; some cyber
policies include it, others sell it separately.

PCI fines and assessments:
Penalties imposed by payment card brands (Visa, MasterCard,
etc.) when a breach involves card data. Coverage scope and
sub-limits vary by policy.


HOW CYBER POLICIES TRIGGER
===========================

Cyber is almost universally CLAIMS-MADE for the third-party
coverages — the privacy, security, and regulatory liability
parts respond to claims first made during the policy period.
First-party coverages typically respond on a "loss
discovered" basis — coverage applies to incidents the insured
becomes aware of during the policy period, regardless of when
the underlying intrusion began (subject to retroactive date
on the third-party side).

Like other claims-made coverages, retroactive date matters.
"Prior acts" coverage on a new cyber policy preserves
coverage for incidents that occurred before the new policy
but are discovered after.


THE RANSOMWARE EVOLUTION
=========================

The cyber market has reshaped substantially since the late
2010s as ransomware grew from nuisance to systemic risk.

What changed:
- Average ransom demands grew from thousands to millions
- "Double extortion" emerged (encrypt AND threaten to leak
  data publicly), making refusal to pay riskier
- Critical infrastructure attacks raised regulatory and
  geopolitical stakes
- Some carriers exited the market; others sharply restricted
  capacity
- Premium increased 50%-300% in some years
- Underwriting tightened materially — MFA, EDR, backups
  tested and offline, patching cadence, employee training
  all became expected baseline

Modern underwriting expectations:
- Multi-factor authentication on email, remote access,
  privileged accounts, and any internet-facing systems
- Endpoint detection and response (EDR) deployed across
  endpoints
- Encrypted, offline, regularly tested backups (so
  ransomware-encrypted production data isn't the only copy)
- Network segmentation between IT and OT, between business
  systems and backups
- Email filtering and security awareness training
- Documented incident response plan
- Vulnerability management program
- Privileged access management

Carriers that previously bound coverage on a short
questionnaire now require detailed security assessments,
external scanning, and in some cases penetration test
reports. Insureds without baseline controls may not be
insurable at all in the current market.

Coinsurance and sub-limits:
Ransomware sub-limits have become standard — many policies
cap ransomware coverage at a fraction of the policy limit
(e.g. $1M ransomware sub-limit within a $5M policy). Some
require coinsurance on the ransomware piece. Some exclude
ransom payment entirely while still covering response costs
and BI.


COMMON CYBER EXCLUSIONS
========================

Standard exclusions across most cyber forms:

War and hostile acts:
War, terrorism, and cyber operations attributed to nation-
states. Following the NotPetya litigation (Merck and others
recovered against carriers despite war exclusions, with
courts holding that traditional war exclusions don't
clearly apply to cyber events), carriers have rewritten
cyber war exclusions with more specific language. The
"cyberwar" carve-out is one of the most actively litigated
areas in cyber coverage.

Prior known incidents:
Incidents the insured was aware of before policy inception.

Bodily injury and tangible property damage:
Generally excluded (those belong on CGL and property). Some
cyber policies provide narrow buy-backs for BI/PD arising
from a cyber event.

Patent and trade secret infringement:
Often excluded; sometimes covered with sub-limits.

Contractual liability:
Liability the insured assumed in a contract beyond what
common law would impose — often excluded, with carve-backs
for standard customer/vendor contract liabilities.

Failure to maintain security standards:
If the insured represented at underwriting that certain
controls were in place and they were not, claims arising
from that gap may be excluded. (Coverage rescission for
material misrepresentation is also an issue.)

Acts of insureds:
Intentional acts by senior management; criminal acts.

Infrastructure failures:
Power outages, ISP outages, generalized internet outages —
often excluded from cyber BI (these belong on a broader
business interruption or "infrastructure" coverage if
available).

Unencrypted devices:
Some policies sub-limit or exclude breaches involving
unencrypted laptops/portable devices.

Failure to apply known patches:
Specific exclusions for events caused by failure to apply
publicly available security patches.

Bricking of vehicles, aircraft, vessels:
Cyber events affecting transportation assets typically
require specialized coverage.


HOW CYBER INTERACTS WITH OTHER POLICIES
=========================================

Cyber sits at the intersection of multiple traditional
coverages, creating coordination questions:

Crime policies:
Funds transfer fraud and social engineering overlap with
cyber. Some carriers offer cyber-included; others rely on
the crime policy. Coverage gaps can arise when both
policies have sub-limits or exclude what the other covers.

Property policies:
Most property policies have cyber exclusions, but some
ambiguity remained on physical damage caused by cyber
events (NotPetya again). Industry standard cyber exclusion
language is now generally clear that cyber events shift to
cyber policies.

CGL:
The data breach itself isn't typically BI/PD. Privacy
liability in CGL has been pushed out via exclusion. Some
narrow CGL coverage (Coverage B, personal and advertising
injury) historically arguable for privacy claims; carriers
have largely closed that.

D&O:
Failure-of-oversight derivative suits following a breach
(directors allegedly failed to oversee cyber risk) are D&O
claims, not cyber. Cyber policies don't cover director
liability for governance failures.

Tech E&O:
Integrated tech E&O + cyber policies are common — a
software/IT services company has both professional errors
exposure and cyber breach exposure, often covered on a
combined form.

Kidnap and Ransom (K&R):
Ransomware sits awkwardly between cyber and K&R. Most
ransomware claims flow through cyber; some specialty K&R
forms address it as well.


HIGH-PROFILE REGULATORY FRAMEWORKS
====================================

Cyber claims are shaped by overlapping regulatory regimes:

HIPAA (US health information):
Triggers OCR investigations and breach notification
obligations for healthcare entities and business
associates.

GLBA (US financial information):
Financial institution privacy and security requirements,
state-administered enforcement.

State breach notification laws:
All 50 US states have breach notification statutes. Timing,
content, and recipient requirements vary substantially. A
multi-state breach triggers parallel notification
obligations under each affected state's law.

CCPA / CPRA (California):
Private right of action for breaches of certain personal
information, statutory damages per consumer per incident.

GDPR (European Union):
Applies to data of EU residents regardless of where the
processor is located. Fines up to 4% of global annual
turnover. 72-hour breach notification.

State comprehensive privacy laws:
A growing patchwork — California, Virginia, Colorado,
Connecticut, Utah, and many others have enacted broad
privacy regimes with breach implications.

Sector-specific:
SEC cybersecurity disclosure rules for public companies,
NY DFS Cybersecurity Regulation (financial institutions),
FERC/NERC for energy sector, and many more.

Cyber policies typically cover regulatory defense across
these frameworks; coverage of FINES specifically depends on
the policy and on local law on fine insurability.


WHO BUYS CYBER
===============

Practically every business with internet-connected systems
and any sensitive data has cyber exposure. Higher-risk
profiles include:

- Healthcare (PHI under HIPAA)
- Financial services (PCI, GLBA, state regulators)
- Retail (cardholder data, customer PII)
- Technology / SaaS (data of customers' customers, dependency
  exposure for downstream BI claims)
- Professional services (client confidential data)
- Manufacturing (OT/ICS exposure, ransomware downtime cost)
- Education (student data, large user populations)
- Government and government contractors (sensitive data, FAR
  and DFARS cybersecurity requirements)
- Critical infrastructure (energy, water, transport)

Cyber adoption among small business has grown rapidly as
small-business ransomware claims have risen and as
contractual cyber requirements (often imposed by larger
customers) have spread.


KEY TAKEAWAYS
=============

- Cyber is a multi-coverage product splitting first-party
  (insured's own losses) from third-party (liability to
  others)
- First-party costs (incident response, BI, extortion) often
  exceed third-party liability on a given incident
- The market has hardened substantially around ransomware,
  with controls expectations now central to underwriting
- Coordination with crime, property, D&O, tech E&O, and K&R
  policies matters — gaps and overlaps are common
- Cyber war exclusions, ransomware sub-limits, and waiting
  periods are major coverage-quality differentiators
- The regulatory landscape (HIPAA, GDPR, state privacy laws)
  drives much of the third-party exposure and defense cost
