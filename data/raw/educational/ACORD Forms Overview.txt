ACORD Forms: What They Are and How They're Used
================================================

This document explains what ACORD forms are, who controls them, and
where they fit into the insurance transaction lifecycle. This is a
companion to the forms collection (which catalogs specific national
ACORD forms by number); this doc covers the conceptual layer.


WHAT IS ACORD?
==============

ACORD (Association for Cooperative Operations Research and
Development) is a non-profit standards organization for the insurance
industry. Founded in 1970, ACORD develops and maintains a set of
standardized forms, data definitions, and electronic transaction
standards that allow agents, carriers, and customers to exchange
insurance information in a consistent format across the industry.

ACORD's mission is to reduce friction in insurance transactions.
Before standardization, every carrier had its own application forms,
claim forms, and policy administration forms. An independent agent
representing 10 carriers had to learn 10 different formats for the
same underlying task. ACORD's forms are the lingua franca that makes
multi-carrier agency operations practical.

ACORD itself is funded by members — primarily insurance carriers,
agents, brokers, and technology providers. Membership funds the
ongoing development, maintenance, and revision of forms and
standards.


WHAT IS AN ACORD FORM?
======================

An ACORD form is a standardized document for a specific insurance
transaction, identified by a number (and sometimes a letter suffix)
and an edition date. The form number identifies what the form is for;
the edition date identifies which version is current.

Example: ACORD 25 (2025-12) — Certificate of Liability Insurance.
- "ACORD 25" identifies the form (Certificate of Liability Insurance)
- "(2025-12)" identifies the edition (December 2025 revision)

ACORD numbering follows rough conventions:
- 1-19: claim and loss notice forms (Property Loss Notice, Automobile
  Loss Notice, Liability Notice of Occurrence, etc.)
- 20-31: certificates and evidence (Certificate of Liability
  Insurance, Evidence of Property Insurance, etc.)
- 35-58: policy administration (Cancellation Request, Notice of
  Cancellation, Auto ID Cards, Financial Responsibility filings)
- 60s: supplements and special notices (Flood Selection, Terrorism
  Coverage Offerings, etc.)
- 70s: change requests (Personal Auto Policy Change Request, etc.)
- 80-89: personal lines applications (Homeowner, Personal Inland
  Marine, Watercraft, Personal Umbrella, etc.)
- 125+: commercial line-of-business sections (Applicant Information,
  CGL, Business Auto, Workers Comp, Property, BOP)
- 325-342: aviation forms (Aircraft Section, Pilot Experience, etc.)
- 401-410: agriculture / farm
- 501-504: surety bonds
- 801-877: specialty lines (E&O, D&O, Cyber, Professional Liability,
  Background Check, etc.)


WHO CONTROLS ACORD FORMS?
=========================

ACORD itself controls the form templates, numbering system, and
periodic revisions. New editions of forms are released when industry
needs change — for example, cyber liability coverage being added,
TRIA terrorism disclosures being revised, or state law changes being
incorporated.

Individual states do NOT control ACORD forms directly — but state
insurance regulators may require specific information to be captured
on certain forms (e.g. fraud warning language, privacy disclosures,
or financial responsibility filings). ACORD typically integrates
these state-specific requirements into the form so a single form
satisfies most state needs. Where state requirements diverge
significantly, ACORD may publish state-specific variants or
supplements.

Carriers cannot modify ACORD forms unilaterally. They can supplement
ACORD forms with their own proprietary applications and
questionnaires, but the ACORD form itself maintains its standardized
structure.


WHERE ACORD FORMS FIT IN THE TRANSACTION LIFECYCLE
==================================================

ACORD forms appear at every stage of an insurance transaction:

Quote stage:
- Applications (ACORD 80 Homeowner, ACORD 84 Dwelling Fire, ACORD 125
  Commercial Applicant Information, ACORD 130 Workers Comp, etc.)
  capture the information underwriters need to quote a policy.
- Supplements (ACORD 73 Solid Fuel Questionnaire, ACORD 74 Residence-
  Based Business, ACORD 185 Restaurant/Tavern, etc.) add detail for
  unusual exposures.

Bind / Issue stage:
- Binders (ACORD 75, 275, 276) provide temporary evidence of coverage
  while the formal policy is being issued.
- Auto Insurance ID Cards (ACORD 50, 50WM, 50WMSET) are issued at
  policy inception so the insured can demonstrate coverage to law
  enforcement.

Active policy:
- Certificates (ACORD 25 Liability, ACORD 24 Property, ACORD 30
  Garage, ACORD 31 Marine/Energy, etc.) provide proof of insurance to
  third parties — vendors, lessors, contracting parties.
- Evidence forms (ACORD 27 Personal Property, ACORD 28 Commercial
  Property, ACORD 29 Flood) provide ongoing proof to mortgagees and
  lenders.

Mid-term changes:
- Change requests (ACORD 70 Personal Non-Auto, ACORD 71 Personal
  Auto, ACORD 175 Commercial) document insured-requested changes to
  a policy.
- Agent of Record changes (ACORD 36) transfer servicing authority.

Claims:
- Loss notices (ACORD 1 Property, 2 Automobile, 3 Liability, 4
  Workers Comp, 5 Aircraft) file First Notice of Loss.
- Supplemental schedules (ACORD 6 Aviation Witness/Passenger, ACORD 7
  Aviation Injured) provide additional claim detail.

End / Cancellation:
- Cancellation Request (ACORD 35) documents an insured-initiated
  cancellation.
- Notice of Cancellation or Termination (ACORD 58) is the carrier's
  cancellation notice.
- Financial Responsibility Cancellation (ACORD 57) ends an active
  SR-22 filing.


WHAT'S USUALLY IN A DEC PACKAGE?
================================

A "dec package" — the packet of documents an insured receives when a
policy is bound or renewed — typically contains:

- The carrier's policy declarations page itself (NOT an ACORD form —
  this is carrier-proprietary). The dec page summarizes named
  insured, policy period, premium, coverage limits, deductibles,
  applicable endorsements, and additional interests.
- Auto ID cards (ACORD 50 or 50WM) for auto policies.
- Evidence forms (ACORD 27, 28, or 29) for property policies with a
  mortgagee.
- Certificates (ACORD 25 or 24) if the insured requested them, or
  needs to provide them to third parties.
- Carrier-specific forms, endorsements, and notices (privacy notices,
  state-required disclosures, etc.) that aren't ACORD forms but are
  attached to the policy.

ACORD forms in the dec package are typically the certificates, ID
cards, and evidence forms — proof documents the insured will use or
distribute. The carrier's policy form itself (the actual contract
language) is not an ACORD form — it's the carrier's own contract or
an ISO/AAIS coverage form licensed to the carrier.


WHAT'S USUALLY IN A QUOTE PACKAGE?
==================================

A quote package — the documents an agent submits to a carrier to
request a quote — typically contains:

- The relevant ACORD application(s) for the lines of business being
  quoted (ACORD 80 Homeowner, ACORD 125 Commercial Applicant
  Information plus line-specific sections like 126 CGL, 127 Business
  Auto, etc.)
- Supporting supplements for unusual exposures (ACORD 186 Contractors
  Supplement, ACORD 185 Restaurant/Tavern, etc.)
- Loss runs from prior carriers (often requested via ACORD 611)
- Replacement cost worksheets (ACORD 42) for property
- Schedule of values (ACORD 139) for multi-location commercial
- Any carrier-specific supplemental questionnaires

Quote packages are almost entirely ACORD-based on the agent side —
that's the whole point of standardization. Different carriers will
respond with their own quote letters, proposals, and binders, but the
information going INTO the carrier comes in standardized ACORD
format.


ACORD FORMS vs. CARRIER FORMS vs. ISO FORMS
============================================

It's worth distinguishing three types of standardized insurance
documents you'll encounter:

ACORD forms: standardized administrative and transactional forms
(applications, certificates, change requests, claim notices). ACORD-
controlled, used across carriers.

ISO / AAIS forms: standardized coverage forms — the actual policy
language defining what is and isn't covered. ISO (Insurance Services
Office) and AAIS (American Association of Insurance Services) develop
these. Examples: CG 00 01 (Commercial General Liability Coverage
Form), CA 00 01 (Business Auto Coverage Form), CP 00 10 (Building and
Personal Property Coverage Form).

Carrier forms: proprietary forms a carrier uses in addition to (or
instead of) ACORD/ISO. These vary by carrier — some carriers use
almost entirely ACORD; others have extensive proprietary forms for
specialty lines.

A typical policy will have:
- An ACORD application captured at the front (administrative)
- ISO or AAIS coverage forms as the policy contract (substantive)
- Carrier-specific endorsements modifying coverage (substantive)
- ACORD certificates / ID cards / evidence forms issued for use
  during the policy period (administrative)

ACORD doesn't define WHAT is covered. ACORD defines HOW information
about the policy and its administration is exchanged.
