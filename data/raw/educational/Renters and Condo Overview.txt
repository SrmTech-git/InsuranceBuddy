Renters (HO-4) and Condo (HO-6) Insurance
==========================================

This document explains the two homeowners forms designed for
insureds who don't own the building they live in — renters (HO-4)
and condominium unit owners (HO-6). Both are members of the
homeowners family but cover materially less than HO-3 because
someone else (landlord or condo association) is responsible for
the building itself.


HO-4 — RENTERS / TENANTS COVERAGE
==================================

What it is:
HO-4 (Contents Broad Form) is the homeowners form for tenants —
people who rent an apartment, house, or other dwelling. It covers
the tenant's belongings, liability, and loss of use, but NOT the
building.

Why renters need it:
The landlord's policy covers the building only. It does NOT cover
the tenant's personal property and does NOT defend the tenant
against liability claims. A tenant without renters insurance is
exposed to:
- Loss of furniture, electronics, clothing, etc. from fire,
  theft, water damage, vandalism
- Liability if a guest is injured in the rented unit
- Liability if the tenant causes damage to the building (a
  kitchen fire spreading to neighboring units)
- Cost of temporary housing if the unit is uninhabitable

Coverage parts on HO-4:

Coverage C — Personal Property:
The core coverage. Pays for damage to the tenant's belongings on
a named-perils basis (the standard HO peril list: fire,
lightning, windstorm, hail, smoke, theft, vandalism, etc.).
Limit selected at quoting based on an inventory of contents —
typical range $15K-$50K for an apartment, more for a furnished
house.

Sub-limits apply to specific categories (jewelry, electronics,
firearms, business property, cash) — same structure as HO-3.

Coverage D — Loss of Use:
Pays additional living expense if the rented unit is uninhabitable
after a covered loss. Typically a percentage of Coverage C.

Coverage E — Personal Liability:
Pays for bodily injury or property damage the tenant is legally
liable for. Includes liability for fire damage to the landlord's
property (the "fire legal liability" exposure). Common limits
$100K-$500K. Defense costs in addition.

Coverage F — Medical Payments:
No-fault medical payments for guests injured at the unit. Common
limits $1K-$5K.

What HO-4 does NOT cover:
- The building itself (the landlord's responsibility)
- Tenant improvements that become part of the realty (in some
  forms)
- Roommate's belongings (each roommate needs their own policy)
- Flood and earthquake (separate coverage)

Replacement cost vs ACV:
HO-4 contents default to ACV but can be upgraded to replacement
cost — strongly recommended given the typical age of tenant
belongings.


HO-6 — CONDO / UNIT-OWNERS COVERAGE
====================================

What it is:
HO-6 is the homeowners form for condominium unit owners and
cooperative apartment owners. It bridges the gap between what the
condo association's master policy covers (the building shell)
and what the unit owner is responsible for (everything inside).

Coverage parts on HO-6:

Coverage A — Building / Improvements and Betterments:
Covers the unit owner's interest in:
- Improvements and betterments inside the unit (cabinets,
  flooring, fixtures the unit owner installed or paid for)
- Items not covered by the master policy (varies by master
  policy type — see below)
- The unit's interior walls in some master policy structures

Coverage A limits on HO-6 are typically much lower than on HO-3
($20K-$100K is common) because the master policy carries the bulk
of the building exposure.

Coverage C — Personal Property:
Contents — furniture, clothing, electronics. Same structure as
HO-4 / HO-3 Coverage C.

Coverage D — Loss of Use:
ALE if the unit is uninhabitable.

Coverage E — Personal Liability:
Liability for the unit owner's activities. Some exposures the
association handles via the master policy, but personal liability
(slip-and-fall in your unit, dog bite by your dog, etc.) is on
the unit owner.

Coverage F — Medical Payments to Others:
Same as HO-3 Coverage F.

Loss Assessment Coverage:
A coverage unique to condo policies. If the association levies a
special assessment on unit owners to cover a loss the master
policy didn't fully cover (a deductible, a coverage gap, or
liability above the master limit), Loss Assessment pays the unit
owner's share, up to the limit. Default is often $1,000; commonly
increased to $10K-$50K.


MASTER POLICY TYPES — WHAT THE ASSOCIATION COVERS
==================================================

The HO-6 unit owner's Coverage A limit depends on what the condo
association's master policy covers. There are three common
structures:

Bare Walls (least association coverage):
The master policy covers only the building structure — exterior
walls, roof, common areas. The unit owner is responsible for
everything inside the unit walls: drywall, paint, flooring,
fixtures, cabinets, appliances. The unit owner needs a HIGH
Coverage A limit on HO-6.

Single Entity / Standard (middle ground):
The master policy covers the original construction as built —
including standard fixtures, flooring, cabinets, etc. — but NOT
upgrades the unit owner has made. The unit owner needs Coverage A
mainly for upgrades and improvements (and may need significant
limits if they've remodeled).

All-In / All-Inclusive (most association coverage):
The master policy covers the original construction AND any
upgrades/improvements the unit owner has made. The unit owner
needs the LEAST Coverage A on HO-6 — just enough for any items
the master excludes (sometimes interior decorations).

Determining which type:
The condo association's master policy declarations or the
condominium bylaws/CC&Rs specify what the master covers. The
agent should obtain the master policy declarations when writing
HO-6 to size Coverage A appropriately.


COMMON HO-4 / HO-6 ENDORSEMENTS
================================

Scheduled Personal Property (HO 04 61):
Same as HO-3 — schedules high-value items individually beyond
Coverage C sub-limits.

Water Backup (HO 04 95):
Covers sewer/drain backup damage to the insured's property — not
covered by the base form.

Replacement Cost on Contents:
Upgrades Coverage C from ACV to replacement cost.

Identity Theft:
Same as HO-3.

Earthquake / Flood:
Same exclusions as HO-3; same separate-policy or endorsement
solutions.

Increased Loss Assessment (HO-6 only):
Raises the loss assessment limit above the default $1,000.

Pet Liability:
Some carriers exclude or sub-limit dog liability; endorsements
restore or limit coverage.

Home Business / Incidental Business:
Covers limited business activity from the residence — base form
excludes most business pursuits.


COMMON EXCLUSIONS (HO-4 AND HO-6)
==================================

Same as HO-3 base exclusions:
- Flood
- Earthquake
- War
- Wear and tear
- Intentional acts
- Business pursuits beyond narrow exceptions
- Mold (often sub-limited)
- Vacancy beyond stated period


WHO IS A NAMED INSURED
======================

The tenant (HO-4) or unit owner (HO-6) is the named insured.
Resident family members are covered automatically. Roommates who
aren't related are NOT covered — they need their own policy.
Some carriers allow naming a roommate as an additional insured
on the same policy; many don't, and separate policies are usually
cleaner.


COMMON CLAIMS SCENARIOS
=======================

HO-4 / renters:
- Theft of laptop, electronics, bike
- Water damage from a unit upstairs leaking into the rented unit
- Kitchen fire — coverage for tenant's contents and for fire
  legal liability against the landlord's building
- Hotel stay after a building fire forces evacuation
- Dog bite — guest injured at the unit

HO-6 / condo:
- Burst pipe damaging the unit's flooring and cabinets — HO-6
  Coverage A pays the unit owner's portion, master policy pays
  the building portion
- Special assessment for a roof replacement after a storm exceeds
  the master policy limit — Loss Assessment pays the unit
  owner's share
- Liability claim from a guest slipping in the unit


WHEN HO-4 / HO-6 IS NOT THE RIGHT FIT
======================================

- Tenant of a single-family rental house who's responsible for
  significant exterior maintenance: still HO-4, but make sure the
  lease doesn't shift building exposure to the tenant
- Subletter or short-term tenant: most HO-4 forms require the
  tenant to be the primary occupant for some duration; short-
  term arrangements may need a different form
- Cooperative apartment in NY: HO-6 with co-op-specific
  endorsements; the structure is similar but the legal interest
  differs (shares in the cooperative rather than fee ownership)
- Condo used as a rental property (landlord, not owner-occupant):
  use a DP form or a Condo Rental Unit Owner policy, not HO-6
- College student living in a dorm: often covered as personal
  property of a resident family member under the parents' HO-3
  Coverage C (up to a sub-limit) — separate HO-4 may not be
  needed
