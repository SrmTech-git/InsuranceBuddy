Personal Auto Insurance: Policy Structure and Coverage
=======================================================

This document explains how the Personal Auto Policy (PAP) is
structured, what each coverage part does, who counts as an insured,
what's typically excluded, and the common endorsements that
modify the standard contract.


WHAT THE PAP IS
===============

The Personal Auto Policy is the standard contract for individual
and family-owned private passenger vehicles. ISO publishes the
model PAP form (PP 00 01); most carriers either use the ISO form
directly or use a substantially similar proprietary form. The PAP
is one contract with multiple coverage "Parts" — each part covers
a different aspect of auto exposure.


POLICY STRUCTURE — THE SIX PARTS
=================================

Part A — Liability Coverage:
Pays for bodily injury (BI) and property damage (PD) the insured
becomes legally responsible for. Defense costs are typically paid
in addition to the limit. Limits are written as split limits
(e.g. 100/300/100 = $100K per person BI / $300K per accident BI /
$100K PD per accident) or as a combined single limit (e.g. CSL
$500K). State minimums vary widely — 25/50/25 is a common minimum
floor; 100/300/100 is a common quoted level; serious drivers carry
250/500/100 or higher with an umbrella stacked on top.

Part B — Medical Payments:
Pays medical expenses for the insured and passengers regardless of
fault, up to the per-person limit. Common limits: $1K, $5K,
$10K, $25K. Not required in all states; replaced by PIP in no-
fault states.

Part C — Uninsured / Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM):
Pays the insured's own damages when the at-fault driver has no
insurance (UM) or insufficient insurance (UIM). State law dictates
whether UM/UIM is mandatory, must be offered, can be waived, and
how it stacks across vehicles. Often the most state-variable part
of a PAP.

Part D — Coverage for Damage to Your Auto (physical damage):
Two sub-coverages:
- Collision: damage from impact with another vehicle or object,
  or overturning. Subject to a deductible (typical: $500, $1,000,
  $2,500).
- Other Than Collision (Comprehensive): all other physical damage
  — theft, vandalism, fire, hail, falling objects, animal strike,
  glass breakage. Subject to a deductible (often lower than
  collision; sometimes $0 glass deductible).
Physical damage is optional except when required by a lienholder
or lessor.

Part E — Duties After an Accident or Loss:
The insured's contractual obligations — notify the carrier
promptly, cooperate with investigation, submit to examination
under oath, protect the vehicle from further damage. Failure to
comply can void coverage for that loss.

Part F — General Provisions:
Cancellation, transfer of interest, fraud, legal action against
the company, our right to recover (subrogation), choice of law.
The administrative spine of the contract.


WHO IS AN INSURED
=================

Named insured:
The person(s) listed on the declarations. The named insured is
covered for any covered auto and for any non-owned auto they
operate (subject to exclusions).

Family member:
A resident of the named insured's household related by blood,
marriage, or adoption (includes wards and foster children). Family
members get coverage similar to the named insured.

Anyone using a covered auto:
Permissive users — neighbors, friends, mechanics — are covered
when using the insured's vehicle with permission. Coverage may be
narrower than for the named insured (some carriers exclude or
sub-limit permissive users by endorsement).

Any "covered auto":
The PAP covers vehicles listed on the declarations PLUS:
- Newly acquired autos for a stated period (often 14-30 days,
  varies by carrier and policy form)
- Trailers owned by the insured
- Temporary substitutes when a covered auto is out of service
  for repair

Anyone for whom the insured is legally responsible:
The carrier defends and pays for liability the insured incurs
because of someone else's actions — for example, when a permissive
user causes a loss.


SPLIT LIMITS vs COMBINED SINGLE LIMIT
======================================

Split limits (e.g. 100/300/100):
- $100,000 BI per person
- $300,000 BI per accident (cap regardless of number injured)
- $100,000 PD per accident

Combined Single Limit (e.g. CSL $500K):
A single pool of money available to pay BI and PD combined, with
no per-person or per-coverage sub-limit. Simpler and often more
favorable in a high-injury single-claimant scenario.

State minimums are almost always quoted as split limits. Most
quoted coverage in the personal market is split limits. CSL is
more common on commercial auto.


UM/UIM MECHANICS
================

UM (uninsured motorist):
Pays the insured's damages when the at-fault driver has no
liability insurance, can't be identified (hit-and-run, in many
states), or is otherwise uncollectible.

UIM (underinsured motorist):
Pays the insured's damages when the at-fault driver's liability
limits are exhausted but the insured's damages exceed them.

State variations:
- Mandatory in some states, must-offer in others, fully optional
  in a few
- Some states require UM = liability limit unless waived in
  writing
- "Stacking" of UM across multiple vehicles is allowed in some
  states, prohibited in others
- UIM may be "limit-to-limit" (the UIM limit is reduced by the
  at-fault driver's payment) or "excess" (UIM stacks on top of
  the at-fault driver's payment)

UM/UIM is the most state-variable part of personal auto coverage
— always check the specific state's statutes (in our case, ORC
3937.18 for Ohio and equivalent statutes in other states).


NO-FAULT AND PIP
================

In no-fault states, drivers carry Personal Injury Protection (PIP)
that pays the insured's own medical expenses, lost wages, and
sometimes essential services regardless of fault. PIP limits and
benefits vary widely by state. No-fault doesn't eliminate
liability coverage — it limits when one driver can sue another
(typically only above a "threshold" of injury severity).

States with mandatory PIP / no-fault: FL, HI, KS, KY, MA, MI,
MN, NJ, NY, ND, PA, UT, plus optional/add-on in others.


COMMON PAP EXCLUSIONS
=====================

The PAP excludes (among other things):
- Intentional injury or damage
- Property owned by or transported by the insured (use Part D
  instead)
- Use as a public livery / for-hire carrier (taxi, ridesharing
  for compensation — see TNC endorsement)
- Damage to a vehicle while being raced or in a speed contest
- Damage to a covered auto while leased or rented to others
- Vehicles owned by the insured but not listed on the policy (the
  "owned but not insured" exclusion — prevents an insured from
  carrying coverage on one vehicle and driving an uninsured one
  in the same household)
- Business use beyond commuting and incidental personal-vehicle
  business (use Business Auto for sales/delivery use)
- Nuclear / war
- Damage caused by wear and tear, mechanical failure, road damage
  to tires (covered under Part D if caused by an otherwise
  covered peril)


COMMON ENDORSEMENTS
===================

Towing and Labor:
Reimbursement for towing and on-site labor after a breakdown.
Often capped at $50-$100 per occurrence.

Rental Reimbursement:
Pays for a rental car while a covered auto is being repaired
after a covered loss. Daily limit and maximum (e.g. $40/day, $1,200
max).

Custom Equipment / Special Equipment:
Coverage for aftermarket modifications (sound systems, lift kits,
custom wheels) beyond a base PAP sub-limit.

Named Driver Exclusion:
Excludes a specific named individual from coverage — often used
when a household member has a poor driving record that would
otherwise make the policy uninsurable.

Transportation Network Company (TNC) Endorsement:
Restores coverage during ridesharing app activity (Uber, Lyft).
Standard PAP excludes use as a livery, so without this endorsement
or the TNC's own contingent coverage, there's a coverage gap
during app-on periods.

OEM Parts / Original Equipment Endorsement:
Requires repairs to use OEM parts rather than aftermarket. Useful
for newer or luxury vehicles.

Gap Coverage / Loan-Lease Payoff:
If a financed/leased vehicle is totaled, comprehensive/collision
pays actual cash value — which may be less than the loan balance.
Gap coverage pays the difference. Sometimes carried on the loan
itself rather than the policy.

Roadside Assistance:
Bundles towing, lockout, jump-start, fuel delivery, flat tire
service. May overlap with auto club memberships.

Accident Forgiveness:
Carrier's promise not to surcharge for the first at-fault accident
(carrier-specific feature, not an ISO endorsement).


FINANCIAL RESPONSIBILITY FILINGS
=================================

SR-22:
A state filing the carrier makes on behalf of the insured proving
the insured carries at least state-minimum liability coverage.
Required after certain violations (DUI, repeat offenses, driving
uninsured). The carrier notifies the state if the policy
cancels — which can trigger license suspension.

FR-44:
Higher-limit version required in Florida and Virginia after
certain serious violations — typically requires double the state
minimum limits.

SR-50 / SR-21:
Less common state-specific filings.


WHEN A PAP IS NOT THE RIGHT FIT
================================

- Vehicles used primarily for business: Business Auto Policy
- Commercial fleets: Business Auto / fleet program
- Vehicles owned by a corporation or LLC: typically Business Auto
  (even if used personally)
- Motorcycles, ATVs, snowmobiles: motorcycle policy or recreational
  vehicle policy — not the PAP
- Antique/classic vehicles driven occasionally: classic car
  policies with agreed value
- Rideshare drivers regularly app-on: TNC-specific policy or
  commercial auto, not just an endorsement
- Drivers without a home address / "non-owner" coverage: non-
  owner policy (covers liability only, when driving others'
  vehicles)
