Insurance Market Structure and Regulation
==========================================

This document explains how the US insurance market is organized —
the types of carriers that write business, how they're regulated,
the difference between admitted and surplus lines, the safety nets
that protect consumers when carriers fail, and the rating agencies
that grade carrier financial strength. This is the "who and how"
layer that sits above any specific policy or coverage.


THE CENTRAL FACT: INSURANCE IS STATE-REGULATED
===============================================

Insurance in the United States is regulated primarily by the
states, not by the federal government. This was settled by the
McCarran-Ferguson Act of 1945, which gave states the authority to
regulate the business of insurance and exempted insurance from
most federal antitrust law (so long as the states regulate it).

Practical consequences:
- Each state has its own insurance department (sometimes called
  the Department of Insurance, Division of Insurance, or
  Insurance Commissioner's office)
- Each state licenses its own carriers, agents, and brokers
- Each state approves its own rates and policy forms (or chooses
  not to — file-and-use vs prior-approval regimes)
- Each state runs its own guaranty fund
- Each state defines its own minimum coverage requirements (auto
  liability limits, workers comp scope, etc.)

A carrier wanting to write in all fifty states needs licensing,
rate filings, and ongoing compliance in all fifty — a substantial
operational lift, which is one reason many carriers focus
regionally.


THE NAIC — COORDINATION, NOT REGULATION
========================================

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) is a
voluntary organization of the state insurance commissioners. The
NAIC does NOT regulate carriers directly — it has no statutory
authority. What it does:

- Develops "model laws" and "model regulations" that states can
  adopt to harmonize regulation
- Operates the NAIC accreditation program, which sets minimum
  standards for state insurance department operations
- Maintains shared financial data systems used by state regulators
- Runs IRIS (Insurance Regulatory Information System) — financial
  ratios used to flag carriers for additional scrutiny
- Coordinates multi-state market conduct examinations

The NAIC is the closest thing to national insurance regulation,
but it operates by persuasion and adoption rather than mandate.
When you see something described as "the NAIC model" — that's a
template, not a binding rule, until a state adopts it.


CARRIER TYPES
=============

Insurance carriers come in several legal and structural forms:

Stock insurers:
Owned by shareholders, profit-motivated, capital raised by issuing
stock. The most common structure for large commercial and personal
lines carriers. Examples: most publicly traded insurers.

Mutual insurers:
Owned by their policyholders. Surplus belongs to policyholders;
"dividends" paid to policyholders are technically refunds of
overpaid premium. Tend to be conservative in underwriting and
investment, often regionally focused. Common in farm and
agricultural lines, some auto and life carriers.

Reciprocal exchanges:
An unincorporated association of subscribers (policyholders) who
exchange insurance with each other through an attorney-in-fact
that runs the operation. Less common but present in personal auto
and some specialty lines. Subscribers share in profits and may be
assessable for losses depending on the form.

Lloyd's syndicates:
Lloyd's of London is a marketplace, not a single carrier.
Underwriting syndicates — backed by corporate capital and
individual "Names" — write risks in the Lloyd's market. Lloyd's
is dominant in specialty, marine, aviation, and large complex
risks. (See the companion Distribution and Reinsurance overview
for more on how Lloyd's actually operates.)

Captive insurers:
Insurance companies formed to write the risks of their parent
organization or a defined group:
- Single-parent (pure) captives: insure only the parent
- Group captives: insure a group of related companies sharing
  risk
- Cell captives: rented "cells" within a sponsored captive,
  each cell isolated from the others
- Risk Retention Groups (RRGs): federally authorized group
  captives under the Liability Risk Retention Act, restricted to
  liability lines, licensed in one state but able to operate in
  all states

Captives let larger insureds self-insure in a tax-efficient,
ring-fenced structure. Bermuda, Vermont, Cayman, and Hawaii are
common captive domiciles.

Self-insurance:
Not technically insurance — the entity simply pays its own losses
out of operating funds or a dedicated reserve. Common for workers
comp and general liability among large employers. Often paired
with stop-loss reinsurance for catastrophic claims.


DOMESTIC, FOREIGN, AND ALIEN INSURERS
======================================

Carrier domicile terminology:

Domestic:
A carrier domiciled in the state where the policy is being
written. (Domestic in Ohio = chartered in Ohio.)

Foreign:
A carrier domiciled in another US state. (Foreign in Ohio = an
Illinois-chartered carrier writing in Ohio.)

Alien:
A carrier domiciled outside the US. (Alien in Ohio = a Bermuda
or UK carrier writing in Ohio.)

The terminology is from the state's perspective — the same
carrier is "domestic" in its home state and "foreign" everywhere
else.


ADMITTED vs NON-ADMITTED (SURPLUS LINES / E&S)
===============================================

The single most important market distinction. Every carrier
writing in a state is either admitted or non-admitted.

Admitted carriers:
- Hold a Certificate of Authority from the state
- File rates and forms with the state (for approval or
  notification depending on the regime)
- Subject to ongoing financial solvency oversight by the state
- Policyholders are covered by the state guaranty fund if the
  carrier becomes insolvent
- Bound by the state's policy form approvals (less flexibility)
- Subject to market conduct examinations

Non-admitted carriers (surplus lines / Excess and Surplus / E&S):
- Not licensed in the state but eligible to write surplus lines
  business (they appear on the state's "white list" of approved
  surplus lines carriers)
- Free to use their own policy forms and set their own rates
- Can write risks the admitted market won't (unusual, hazardous,
  high-limit, or simply outside admitted carrier appetite)
- NOT backed by the state guaranty fund — this is the central
  consumer trade-off
- Subject to a surplus lines tax (state-specific, typically 3%-6%
  of premium)
- Often domiciled offshore or in a state friendly to surplus
  lines carriers (London, Bermuda, certain US domiciles)

When business goes to surplus lines:
Most states require a "diligent search" of the admitted market
before a risk can be placed with surplus lines. The surplus
lines broker documents declinations from typically three or more
admitted carriers, attesting that the risk could not be placed
in the admitted market on reasonable terms. Some classes of
risk are pre-approved for surplus lines without a diligent
search ("export list" — risks the regulator has acknowledged
won't find admitted capacity).

Why surplus lines exists:
The admitted market is constrained by its own form and rate
filings — a carrier can't easily underwrite a one-off complex
risk without a corresponding filing. Surplus lines fills the
gap with flexible underwriting and bespoke wording. Most
specialty, high-hazard, and large commercial risks ride on
surplus lines paper.

Surplus lines tax:
The surplus lines broker collects the tax from the insured and
remits it to the state. Under the Nonadmitted and Reinsurance
Reform Act (NRRA, 2010), the insured's HOME STATE collects 100%
of the tax for multi-state risks — eliminating earlier multi-
state allocation disputes.


HOW CARRIERS GET LICENSED — CERTIFICATE OF AUTHORITY
=====================================================

To become admitted in a state, a carrier must:
- Demonstrate minimum capital and surplus (varies by line of
  business and state — typically several million dollars per
  line)
- File a plan of operations
- Submit policy forms and rates for approval (or notification,
  depending on regime)
- Designate a registered agent in the state for service of
  process
- Submit ongoing financial reports (annual and quarterly
  statements in NAIC format)
- Pay licensing and renewal fees

Rate and form approval regimes vary:
- Prior approval: file and wait for affirmative regulator OK
  before using
- File and use: file and use immediately, subject to disapproval
- Use and file: use immediately and file shortly after
- No-file / open competition: rate and form flexibility with
  limited regulator review (more common for commercial lines)

Workers comp, personal auto, and homeowners tend toward prior
approval. Large commercial lines tend toward more flexibility.


FINANCIAL SOLVENCY OVERSIGHT
=============================

State regulators monitor admitted carrier solvency through:

Risk-Based Capital (RBC):
A formula that calculates a carrier's required minimum capital
based on the risk profile of its assets, underwriting, and
operations. If actual capital falls below specified RBC ratios,
the regulator escalates intervention — from company-level
corrective action plans to mandatory rehabilitation to
liquidation.

Statutory accounting (SAP):
Insurance carriers use Statutory Accounting Principles for
regulatory reporting, distinct from GAAP. SAP is more
conservative — it focuses on policyholder protection rather than
investor information. Reserves are stricter, certain assets are
non-admitted (can't be counted toward surplus).

NAIC IRIS ratios:
Twelve financial ratios calculated annually for every admitted
carrier. Carriers with ratios outside "usual" ranges get
flagged for further regulator attention.

Market conduct examinations:
Periodic regulatory reviews of how a carrier handles claims,
underwriting, policyholder communications, and complaints —
not just whether it's solvent, but whether it's treating
policyholders fairly.


GUARANTY FUNDS — THE SAFETY NET
================================

If an admitted carrier becomes insolvent, the state's guaranty
fund (sometimes called a guaranty association) steps in to pay
covered claims up to statutory limits. Every state has at least
one (some have separate funds for property/casualty, life/health,
and workers comp).

How it works:
- Guaranty funds are funded by post-insolvency assessments on
  remaining admitted carriers in the state, pro-rated by market
  share
- The fund picks up unpaid claims of the insolvent carrier, up
  to per-claim caps (typically $300K for most P&C claims,
  unlimited for workers comp benefits in most states)
- Refund of unearned premium also covered, often capped at
  $25K-$50K
- High-net-worth individuals may have claims exceeding caps and
  bear the loss themselves above the cap

What guaranty funds do NOT cover:
- Surplus lines policies (the consumer trade-off for the
  flexibility)
- Self-insurance, captives, RRGs (each has its own
  framework)
- Some specialty programs (ocean marine, certain reinsurance
  arrangements)
- Claims above statutory caps

Guaranty fund coverage is one major reason admitted-market
placement matters for risks where the consumer needs that
backstop — personal lines, small commercial, and any contract
where minimum rating requirements or guaranty-fund coverage are
specified.


RESIDUAL AND SHARED MARKETS
============================

When a risk can't get coverage in the voluntary admitted market
(too hazardous, too large, geographically constrained), the
state often provides an insurer of last resort. These are
collectively called residual markets or shared markets.

Auto assigned-risk plans:
Every state has a mechanism to insure drivers who can't get
coverage voluntarily — typically an assigned-risk plan that
allocates these drivers to admitted carriers in proportion to
their market share. Premiums are higher than voluntary market.

FAIR plans:
Fair Access to Insurance Requirements plans provide property
insurance in geographic areas where the voluntary market has
withdrawn (urban areas, wildfire zones, brush areas). Coverage
is typically named-perils, more limited than standard
homeowners. Most US states have FAIR plans for residential and
sometimes commercial property.

Beach and Windstorm Pools:
Coastal states (TX, NC, SC, FL, AL, MS) have specialized pools
covering wind/hail in hurricane-exposed coastal counties where
voluntary carriers won't write the wind peril.

Workers comp residual market:
Every state has a mechanism to insure employers who can't get
voluntary workers comp coverage — either through the NCCI
National Workers Compensation Reinsurance Pool (in NCCI
states), through state-specific pools, or through state-
operated funds. In a few states (e.g. North Dakota, Ohio,
Washington, Wyoming), the state itself is the only workers
comp insurer — these are "monopolistic" state funds.

Residual markets function as insurer of last resort. Premiums
are generally higher than voluntary market and coverage may be
narrower. The existence of a functioning residual market is part
of what makes mandatory insurance requirements (auto, workers
comp) workable — there's always somewhere to place coverage.


RATING AGENCIES AND FINANCIAL STRENGTH
=======================================

Carrier financial strength is graded by independent rating
agencies. The major P&C rating agencies:

A.M. Best:
The dominant rating agency for insurance, focused exclusively
on the insurance industry. Ratings run from A++ (Superior) down
through A+, A, A-, B++, B+, B, B-, C++, C+, C, C-, D, E, F. Most
admitted carriers carry an A- or better. Best's also publishes
a Financial Size Category (FSC) from I to XV, indicating
adjusted policyholders' surplus.

Standard & Poor's, Moody's, Fitch:
General-purpose rating agencies that also rate insurers.
Ratings parallel their corporate ratings — AAA, AA, A, BBB,
etc.

Demotech:
A rating agency focused on smaller and regional carriers,
particularly significant in Florida property where the voluntary
market relies heavily on Demotech-rated carriers.

Why ratings matter in practice:
- Mortgage requirements: many mortgages require the homeowners
  policy to be from an A- or better rated carrier
- Contractual requirements: commercial contracts often specify
  minimum ratings (e.g. "Best's A-VII or better")
- Lender approvals on financed property
- Government contract qualification
- Public entity coverage requirements

A carrier whose rating drops can lose substantial business —
agents may be required to move accounts off downgraded paper to
maintain contractual compliance.


PUTTING IT TOGETHER — A SIMPLE MENTAL MODEL
============================================

When evaluating a risk, the questions in order are:
1. Will the admitted market write it? (preferred — rate
   approval, form approval, guaranty fund coverage)
2. If not, can surplus lines write it? (flexibility at the
   cost of guaranty fund protection)
3. If neither, is there a residual market? (assigned risk,
   FAIR, beach/wind pools, state fund)
4. For very large or unique risks: captive, RRG, or self-
   insurance?

The "where the policy is placed" question is upstream of every
specific coverage question — and it determines the regulatory
framework, consumer protections, and pricing dynamics that
apply.
