Insurance Distribution and Reinsurance
=======================================

This document explains how insurance is distributed — the agents,
brokers, wholesalers, and program administrators that connect
buyers with carriers — and how reinsurance works behind the
scenes to spread risk among carriers. This is the "how policies
actually get placed and how the industry stays solvent" layer
that sits beneath any specific policy.


DISTRIBUTION CHANNELS — HOW POLICIES REACH THE BUYER
=====================================================

Insurance reaches consumers through several distinct channels.
The same coverage might be sold five different ways depending
on the carrier's distribution strategy.

Independent agents:
Agencies that represent multiple carriers and place each
client's business with whichever carrier best fits. The
independent agent owns the customer relationship and the
expirations (the right to renew the business). Independent
agents dominate commercial lines and have a strong share of
personal lines in many regions. Common arrangements include
direct appointments with carriers and access to additional
markets through wholesalers and aggregators.

Captive agents:
Agents who represent a single carrier exclusively. The carrier
owns the customer relationship; the agent is essentially a
distribution arm. Common in personal lines — large mass-market
carriers run national captive agent forces. Captive agents
typically can't shop competitors' rates for the client.

Direct writers:
Carriers that sell directly to consumers without an agent
intermediary — through phone, web, mobile app, or company-
employed sales representatives. Common in personal auto (the
"direct" segment of the personal auto market is a substantial
share of premium). Some direct writers also work through
agents in parallel.

Brokers (commercial):
In the commercial market, "broker" usually refers to a retail
producer representing the buyer (the insured), not the carrier.
Commercial brokers shop the market on the client's behalf,
negotiate terms, and place the business with whichever carrier
offers the best fit. Large national and global brokers
(several major firms dominate this space) handle the largest
commercial accounts; mid-market and small commercial work
through smaller regional brokerages.

Managing General Agents (MGAs) and Managing General
Underwriters (MGUs):
Specialized intermediaries that hold underwriting authority
delegated by a carrier. MGAs/MGUs can quote, bind, issue
policies, and sometimes handle claims on the carrier's behalf,
within the parameters of a binding authority agreement. They
specialize by program, line of business, or geography —
allowing carriers to access niche markets without building
in-house expertise. The carrier still bears the underwriting
risk; the MGA earns a commission plus often a profit share
contingent on loss ratio.

Wholesale brokers:
Intermediaries between retail agents/brokers and surplus lines
carriers (and some specialty admitted carriers). The retail
agent doesn't have direct access to the specialty market;
they bring the account to a wholesaler, who shops the surplus
lines and specialty market and places the business. The
wholesaler is paid by the carrier and shares commission with
the retail agent. Wholesale distribution is the backbone of
the surplus lines market.

Program administrators:
A specialized form of MGA running a "program" — a focused
underwriting platform for a specific industry or class
(restaurants, contractors, real estate, professional firms,
tow trucks, etc.). The program administrator has carrier
backing, defined appetite, and often proprietary risk-scoring.
Retail agents access the program directly or through
wholesalers.

Insurtech and digital channels:
Newer entrants combining technology with one of the above
models. Some are direct-to-consumer carriers; some are
digital MGAs writing on traditional carrier paper; some are
agency platforms streamlining the placement process for
small commercial. Distribution increasingly mixes digital
quoting with human service for complex needs.

Bank assurance and affinity programs:
Insurance sold through a bank, credit union, employer, or
affinity group (alumni association, professional society,
auto club). The distributor leverages an existing customer
relationship; the carrier provides the product. Common in
personal lines and some small commercial.


AGENT vs BROKER — THE LEGAL DISTINCTION
========================================

In strict legal terms, "agent" and "broker" describe different
fiduciary relationships:

Agent: represents the carrier. Has legal authority to bind
coverage on the carrier's behalf within the appointment's
limits. Owes a duty of loyalty primarily to the carrier.

Broker: represents the insured (the buyer). Shops the market
on the buyer's behalf. Doesn't have automatic binding
authority — has to procure coverage through carriers.

In practice, US states blur the distinction substantially.
Most states issue a single producer license rather than
separate agent and broker licenses. The same person may
function as agent on one transaction (carrier-appointed,
binding authority) and broker on another (acting for the
client, going to market). Independent agents are usually
"agents" by license but often function as brokers in spirit —
shopping multiple markets for the client.

In commercial and surplus lines, the terminology more often
holds: a "broker" is shopping the market for the insured; an
"underwriter" or "MGA" is the carrier-side counterpart.


THE LONDON MARKET AND LLOYD'S
==============================

Lloyd's of London is not a single insurance company — it's a
specialized marketplace, structured around syndicates that
underwrite risks brought into the market by brokers.

How Lloyd's works:
- Capital is provided by members (corporate members and
  historically individual "Names" — the corporate capital share
  has grown substantially over time)
- Syndicates are formed from member capital, run by managing
  agents, and underwrite specific lines of business
- Brokers bring risks to the market and walk them around
  syndicates to assemble subscriptions
- A "lead" syndicate underwrites first and sets terms;
  "following" syndicates take portions on the same terms
- Lloyd's itself operates as a regulator and central market
  facility — it doesn't underwrite, but it provides oversight,
  central settlement, and the Central Fund backing of
  syndicate solvency

Why Lloyd's matters:
- Specialty, marine, aviation, energy, political risk, and
  large complex risks heavily ride on Lloyd's paper
- For US surplus lines, Lloyd's syndicates are among the
  largest sources of capacity for hard-to-place commercial
  risks
- The "Lloyd's of London" brand carries weight even when the
  actual risk is being underwritten by a US-based syndicate
- Coverholders / binding authorities — US-based MGAs and
  underwriters operate under delegated authority from Lloyd's
  syndicates, allowing local underwriting against syndicate
  capital


REINSURANCE — INSURANCE FOR INSURERS
=====================================

Reinsurance is insurance purchased by an insurance company to
cede a portion of its underwriting risk to another insurer
(the reinsurer). It's how the industry distributes catastrophic
risk and frees up capacity to write more business.

Why carriers buy reinsurance:
- Cap their exposure to any single loss or catastrophe
- Free up surplus to write additional business
- Smooth earnings volatility across years
- Access expertise on unusual risks
- Manage regulatory capital requirements (RBC)

The ceding carrier (the "cedant") still issues the policy and
owes the insured. The reinsurance contract is between the
ceding carrier and the reinsurer; the policyholder typically
has no direct relationship with the reinsurer. From the
insured's perspective, the carrier on the policy is still on
the hook.


TREATY vs FACULTATIVE
======================

Reinsurance comes in two structural forms based on how risks
are assigned:

Treaty reinsurance:
A contract covering a defined book of business — all the
business the cedant writes in a class, line, or geography,
automatically ceded under pre-agreed terms. The reinsurer
takes whatever the cedant writes that meets the treaty
parameters, without individually underwriting each risk.
Efficient for large volumes of similar risks.

Facultative reinsurance:
Risk-by-risk reinsurance. The cedant offers a specific
individual risk to the reinsurer, the reinsurer underwrites
it individually, and either accepts or declines. Used for
unusual risks, risks that exceed treaty capacity, or risks
the treaty excludes. More expensive per dollar of capacity
than treaty.

Most carriers use a combination — treaties for the bulk of
their book and facultative for the outliers.


PROPORTIONAL vs NON-PROPORTIONAL REINSURANCE
=============================================

The other major structural axis is how losses are shared:

Proportional (pro-rata) reinsurance:
The reinsurer shares premiums and losses in proportion. Two
common forms:

- Quota share: cedant cedes a fixed percentage of every risk
  (e.g. 30% quota share — reinsurer takes 30% of premium and
  pays 30% of losses). Simple, predictable, and used to
  manage surplus.
- Surplus share: cedant retains a defined "line" on each
  risk and cedes the excess above that line up to a multiple.
  Variable cession by risk size — small risks fully retained,
  large risks largely ceded.

Non-proportional (excess of loss) reinsurance:
The reinsurer pays only when losses exceed a defined retention
("attachment point"), up to a limit. Common forms:

- Per-risk excess of loss: covers losses above retention on
  each individual risk
- Per-occurrence excess of loss: covers losses above retention
  on each individual loss event (a hurricane, an explosion, a
  liability event affecting multiple policies)
- Aggregate excess of loss / stop-loss: covers when total
  losses for a period exceed an aggregate retention
- Catastrophe (cat) cover: a specialized per-occurrence layer
  triggered by named catastrophe events

Layering is common: a carrier might buy a per-risk XOL above a
$1M retention, a cat cover above $50M per event, and an
aggregate stop-loss above an 85% loss ratio.


THE REINSURANCE MARKET
=======================

Major global reinsurers:
The reinsurance market is concentrated among a relatively small
number of major reinsurers, plus Lloyd's syndicates and a
number of specialty and regional reinsurers. Bermuda is a major
domicile for reinsurance capital, particularly catastrophe
reinsurance. Continental European reinsurers, Lloyd's, and US-
domiciled reinsurers all participate actively.

Retrocession:
Reinsurers themselves buy reinsurance — called retrocession —
to manage their own concentrations. The retrocession market is
smaller and more concentrated than primary reinsurance.

Alternative capital:
A meaningful share of catastrophe reinsurance capacity now
comes from non-traditional sources — catastrophe bonds, ILS
(insurance-linked securities) funds, sidecars — capital from
pension funds, hedge funds, and other institutional investors
seeking returns uncorrelated with broader financial markets.


WHY REINSURANCE AFFECTS THE RETAIL CONSUMER
============================================

Reinsurance is invisible to most insureds, but it has direct
effects on the retail market:

Capacity availability:
When reinsurance is plentiful and cheap, primary carriers can
write more business and offer higher limits. When reinsurance
tightens, primary carriers shrink their appetite — declining
risks, lowering limits, raising rates.

Pricing volatility after catastrophes:
A major hurricane or earthquake can move global reinsurance
pricing significantly. Those increases flow through to primary
carriers, who pass them through to retail rates over time.
Hard markets in property insurance often originate in the
reinsurance layer.

Carrier insolvency exposure:
A reinsurer failure can leave the primary carrier holding the
bag — the primary still owes the insured, but its expected
reinsurance recovery is at risk. This is one reason regulators
monitor reinsurer credit quality and limit the proportion of
a carrier's surplus that can be supported by recoverables from
any single reinsurer.


MARKET CYCLES — HARD vs SOFT MARKETS
=====================================

The insurance market cycles between "hard" and "soft"
conditions:

Soft market:
- Abundant capital and competition
- Rates flat or declining
- Carriers expanding appetite, taking more classes of risk
- Broader terms, more concessions to clients
- Higher commissions for producers
- New entrants opening

Hard market:
- Constrained capital, often after major losses or investment
  setbacks
- Rates rising, sometimes sharply
- Carriers contracting appetite, declining classes of risk
- Tighter terms, more exclusions, lower limits
- Premium audit and underwriting scrutiny intensify
- Reinsurance pricing leads the way

Cycle drivers:
Major catastrophes deplete industry surplus. Investment
returns affect carriers' need to make underwriting profit.
Reinsurance availability and pricing flow through to primary
carriers. Regulatory and judicial changes (large verdicts,
expanded liability theories) can compress capacity. Capital
inflows from outside the industry can soften markets even
after losses.

What hard markets look like in practice:
- Renewals come back with rate increases of 10%-50%+ on
  certain classes
- Some classes lose admitted-market capacity entirely and
  shift to surplus lines
- Higher attachment points on excess and umbrella layers
- Tightened terms (sub-limits, exclusions, coinsurance
  provisions)
- Specific lines may go through "dislocations" — temporary
  market disruptions in cyber, D&O, habitational property,
  or wildfire-exposed property, for example


PRODUCER COMPENSATION
======================

How agents, brokers, MGAs, and wholesalers get paid:

Commission:
A percentage of premium, paid by the carrier. Standard
commercial commission ranges from 8% to 20% depending on line,
size, and channel. Personal lines commissions vary by carrier
and channel. New business often pays more than renewals.

Contingent commission / profit sharing:
Additional compensation based on the carrier's profitability
on the producer's book. Common in independent agency channels.
Aligns producer incentives with carrier loss experience over
the long term.

Override commission:
Additional commission paid to wholesalers, MGAs, or aggregators
on top of the base commission paid to the retail producer.

Fee-based:
Some large commercial brokers charge the insured a fee for
services rather than (or in addition to) carrier-paid
commission. Increasingly common at the upper end of the
commercial market where the broker is doing substantial
risk-management consulting beyond mere placement.

Disclosure requirements vary by state, but contingent
commission disclosure became more standardized after early-
2000s investigations into broker compensation practices.


PUTTING IT TOGETHER — A SIMPLE MENTAL MODEL
============================================

A commercial policy placed through a retail broker, on
surplus lines paper, supported by reinsurance:

1. Insured engages a retail broker to find coverage
2. Broker can't find admitted appetite, goes to a wholesaler
3. Wholesaler shops surplus lines underwriters — including
   MGAs operating under Lloyd's binding authority and US
   surplus carriers
4. An MGA quotes on behalf of its carrier; broker accepts
5. The MGA's carrier issues the policy
6. The carrier's treaty reinsurance automatically cedes a
   portion of the risk to its reinsurers
7. If the loss exceeds treaty retention or hits an
   excess-of-loss layer, additional reinsurance attaches
8. The insured deals only with the broker; the broker deals
   with the wholesaler; the wholesaler dealt with the MGA;
   the MGA acts for the carrier; the carrier separately
   manages its reinsurance — multiple invisible layers
   between the insured and the ultimate capital paying the
   loss

Distribution and reinsurance together explain why a
seemingly simple commercial policy can involve five or more
parties between the buyer and the capital ultimately bearing
the risk.
