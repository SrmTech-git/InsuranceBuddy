The Claims Process: From First Notice to Closed File
======================================================

This document explains how an insurance claim moves from the
moment of loss through final resolution — who does what, what
documents and decisions appear at each stage, and the common
branches the process can take. It's written conceptually and
deliberately avoids form numbers so it stays useful for general
"how do claims work" questions.

STAGE 1 — THE LOSS EVENT
=========================

A claim starts with a loss — an event that causes damage,
injury, or financial harm potentially covered by an insurance
policy. Common categories:

First-party losses: damage to the insured's own property or
expenses the insured incurs (a kitchen fire, a stolen vehicle,
storm damage, additional living expense while displaced). The
insured is collecting on their own policy.

Third-party losses: the insured allegedly caused harm to
someone else, and that someone else is making a claim or
filing suit. The insured's liability coverage responds to
defend and indemnify (a guest slips on the insured's
property, an at-fault auto accident, a contractor damages
the customer's building).

Workers compensation losses: an employee is injured on the
job. The employer's WC carrier responds to pay medical and
indemnity benefits regardless of fault.

The category drives which coverage part responds and what the
process looks like — first-party claims are an indemnity
transaction with the insured; third-party claims involve a
claimant outside the insured-carrier relationship.


STAGE 2 — FIRST NOTICE OF LOSS (FNOL)
======================================

FNOL is the moment the carrier first learns about the claim.
It can come through several channels:

- Insured calls the carrier directly (800 number, mobile app,
  website)
- Insured reports to the agent, who forwards to the carrier
- Third-party claimant or claimant's attorney contacts the
  carrier
- Carrier learns through litigation (a complaint is served
  before the insured reports)
- Police report or other public record routed to the carrier

What's captured at FNOL:
- Date, time, and location of loss
- Description of what happened
- Property involved or person injured
- Witnesses
- Police or fire report numbers if applicable
- Initial estimate of damage or injury severity
- Insured's contact information and policy number

Why prompt notice matters:
Most policies require "prompt" notice of loss as a condition
of coverage. Unreasonable delay can prejudice the carrier's
investigation rights and, in extreme cases, void coverage for
that claim. Some policies specify timeframes (e.g., notice
within 30 days); others use the "as soon as practicable"
standard. Practically, faster notice means better evidence
preservation, faster investigation, and faster resolution.

For third-party claims, the insured's duty to cooperate
extends beyond initial notice — the insured must forward any
suit papers, demand letters, or correspondence from the
claimant to the carrier immediately. Failure to do so can
jeopardize defense coverage.


STAGE 3 — CLAIM SETUP AND ASSIGNMENT
=====================================

Once the carrier receives FNOL:

Claim number issued:
A unique identifier for tracking the file across the carrier's
systems. The claim number is referenced on every subsequent
document, communication, and payment.

Adjuster assigned:
The adjuster is the carrier representative responsible for
investigating, evaluating, and resolving the claim. Three
common types:

- Staff adjusters: employed directly by the carrier. Most
  routine claims.
- Independent adjusters: contracted by the carrier on a
  per-claim basis. Common for catastrophe response, geographic
  overflow, or specialized loss types.
- Public adjusters: hired by the INSURED, not the carrier, to
  represent the insured's interests in a first-party property
  claim. Public adjusters are licensed separately and paid by
  the insured (usually a percentage of recovery).

Acknowledgment to the insured:
The carrier sends an acknowledgment letter or email confirming
the claim was received, providing the claim number and
adjuster contact info, and outlining what happens next.
Acknowledgment timeframes are often regulated by state unfair
claims practices statutes (typically 10-15 days from FNOL).

Severity triage:
Carriers categorize incoming claims by complexity. Simple,
clear-cut claims (a windshield crack, a minor fender-bender)
may be handled by lower-tier adjusters or even resolved
through self-service. Complex claims (large losses, fatalities,
coverage disputes, suspected fraud) are routed to senior
adjusters or specialty units.


STAGE 4 — INVESTIGATION
========================

The investigation establishes the facts the carrier needs to
make a coverage decision and value the claim.

Coverage analysis:
The adjuster (with management or counsel support on complex
files) compares the alleged loss to the policy:
- Is the loss event a covered cause of loss?
- Is the damaged property or injured party covered?
- Is the loss within the policy period?
- Do any exclusions apply?
- Has the insured complied with policy conditions (prompt
  notice, cooperation, etc.)?

The output of coverage analysis is one of three positions —
covered, uncertain, or not covered — driving the path through
stage 5.

Liability investigation (third-party claims):
Was the insured at fault? To what degree? What's the
comparative fault of other parties? Liability investigation
typically includes:
- Insured's statement
- Claimant's statement (when obtainable)
- Witness statements
- Police reports, accident reconstruction
- Site inspection, photos
- Review of records (driving records, prior claims,
  surveillance video)

Damage assessment (first-party claims):
What was lost? What's it worth?
- Property inspection by the adjuster or a contracted
  estimator
- Itemized inventory for contents losses
- Repair estimates from contractors
- Appraisals for unique items (jewelry, art, classic vehicles)
- For business income claims: financial records establishing
  pre-loss revenue and projected period-of-restoration losses

Statements and records:
Recorded statements are routinely taken from insureds and
claimants in liability claims. For larger or disputed claims,
the carrier may demand an Examination Under Oath (EUO) — a
formal sworn statement, often with counsel present on both
sides. EUOs are most common in suspected fraud cases or
substantial first-party property losses.

Reservation of rights pending investigation:
If the carrier needs to investigate further before deciding
coverage, it may issue a Reservation of Rights letter (see
stage 5) so that any defense or payment provided pending
investigation doesn't waive the carrier's right to deny later
if coverage facts develop differently.

SIU and fraud investigation:
Special Investigations Units (SIU) handle suspected fraud —
red flags include staged losses, inflated claims, claimant
inconsistencies, and patterns across multiple claims. SIU
work runs in parallel to standard adjusting and may involve
surveillance, expert review, and coordinated reporting to
state insurance fraud bureaus.


STAGE 5 — COVERAGE DECISION
============================

Three outcomes:

Covered (clear):
The loss is plainly within coverage. The carrier proceeds to
valuation and payment / defense without coverage caveats.

Coverage uncertain — Reservation of Rights:
The carrier identifies one or more potential coverage issues
but isn't ready to deny. To avoid waiving its right to deny
later, the carrier issues a Reservation of Rights (ROR)
letter:
- Identifies the specific coverage issues
- Confirms the carrier will continue handling (often including
  defense in third-party claims) pending resolution
- Reserves the carrier's right to deny coverage or withdraw
  defense if facts develop adversely
- Sometimes accompanied by a request for the insured to
  acknowledge the ROR

In third-party claims, an ROR may give the insured the right
to independent (insured-chosen) defense counsel paid by the
carrier — the rationale being that carrier-appointed counsel
has a conflict of interest when the carrier is reserving the
right to walk away. State law varies on when independent
counsel is required.

Coverage declaratory judgment:
For high-stakes coverage disputes, either the carrier or the
insured may file a declaratory judgment action — a separate
lawsuit asking a court to rule on whether coverage applies.
Common in complex commercial liability claims.

Not covered — Denial:
The carrier issues a written denial letter:
- States the loss is not covered
- Cites the specific policy language relied on
- Identifies the appeal process and the insured's rights
  (varies by state — often includes the right to complain to
  the state insurance department, the right to litigate, and
  in some states specific internal appeal procedures)

Bad faith exposure:
A wrongful denial — or any unreasonable handling — can expose
the carrier to extra-contractual liability under state bad-
faith laws. Damages can exceed the policy limit and include
emotional distress, attorneys' fees, and in some states
punitive damages. Bad-faith exposure is the structural
incentive for carriers to handle claims thoroughly and fairly
even when the answer is denial.


STAGE 6 — RESERVE SETTING
==========================

A reserve is the carrier's estimate of what the claim will
ultimately cost. Reserves are set early and adjusted as the
file develops.

Components of a reserve:
- Indemnity reserve (the expected payment to insured or
  claimant)
- Defense and cost containment reserve (for third-party
  claims expected to go into litigation)
- Adjuster expense (allocated loss adjustment expense, ALAE)
- Subrogation / salvage recovery offset (estimated)

Why reserves matter operationally:
- Reserves drive carrier financial reporting and reserve
  development is a leading indicator of carrier financial
  health
- Reserves trigger different authority levels — claims above
  certain reserve thresholds require senior approval to
  settle
- Reserve adequacy is part of the loss ratio metrics that
  affect renewal pricing for the underlying policy and the
  carrier's overall book

Reserves are reviewed and updated throughout the life of the
file. A claim that initially looked small ($5K reserve) but
turns into a serious injury claim ($500K+ reserve) gets the
reserve increased as facts develop. This is "reserve
strengthening" or "adverse development."


STAGE 7 — RESOLUTION
=====================

The resolution path depends fundamentally on whether the claim
is first-party or third-party.

FIRST-PARTY RESOLUTION:

Valuation:
- Actual Cash Value (ACV): replacement cost minus depreciation
- Replacement Cost Value (RCV): cost to replace with new
  property of like kind and quality, often paid in two steps
  — ACV first, depreciation holdback paid after actual
  replacement is documented

Proof of loss:
For property claims, a sworn proof of loss formally documents
what was lost and what the insured is claiming. Most policies
require a proof of loss within a specified timeframe (often
60 days from request).

Additional Living Expense (ALE) / Loss of Use:
For homeowners and similar policies, ALE pays the extra cost
of living elsewhere while the home is uninhabitable — hotel,
restaurant meals over normal grocery costs, temporary rental.

Business Income / Time Element:
For commercial property, lost net income and continuing
expenses during the period of restoration are paid under
business income coverage. Calculation requires financial
records demonstrating pre-loss earnings and the period needed
to restore operations.

Appraisal:
When the insured and carrier disagree on the AMOUNT of loss
(not whether it's covered), most property policies include an
appraisal clause. Each side selects an appraiser; the two
appraisers select an umpire; the panel determines the value.
Appraisal binds the amount but does not resolve coverage
disputes.

Payment:
Once the value is agreed, the carrier issues payment. For
property losses with a mortgagee, the check is typically made
payable to both the insured and the mortgagee.

THIRD-PARTY RESOLUTION:

Liability evaluation:
Based on investigation, the carrier evaluates the insured's
exposure: clear liability, comparative fault, defenses
available, jurisdictional damages trends.

Negotiation and settlement:
Most third-party claims settle without litigation. The
adjuster negotiates with the claimant or claimant's counsel,
typically resolving the claim with a release in exchange for
payment. Settlement authority levels are tied to reserves and
internal approval rules.

Suit and defense:
If the claimant files suit (or threatens to), the carrier
appoints defense counsel under its duty to defend. The duty
to defend is broader than the duty to indemnify — the carrier
defends whenever the complaint potentially alleges a covered
claim, even if the underlying facts ultimately turn out not
to be covered.

Verdict or settlement:
Litigation resolves either by settlement (most cases) or by
verdict. Verdicts in excess of policy limits can trigger bad-
faith claims if the carrier had an opportunity to settle
within limits and didn't.

Policy limits demands:
A claimant may make a time-limited demand to settle within
policy limits. Failure to accept a reasonable within-limits
demand can expose the carrier to bad-faith liability for the
entire judgment, including amounts above the policy limit.


STAGE 8 — POST-PAYMENT
=======================

Several activities continue or initiate after the primary
payment.

Subrogation:
If a third party caused the loss, the carrier — having paid
the insured — steps into the insured's shoes and pursues
recovery from the responsible party. Common subrogation
scenarios:
- Auto property damage paid to the insured, then subrogated
  against the at-fault driver's carrier
- Property damage paid for a fire caused by a defective
  product, then subrogated against the manufacturer
- Workers comp benefits paid to an injured employee, then
  subrogated against a third party whose negligence caused
  the injury

Subrogation recoveries reduce the net cost of the claim and
can return the insured's deductible if the carrier recovers
in full.

Waiver of subrogation: many contracts (construction,
commercial leases) require parties to waive subrogation
against each other. When a waiver of subrogation endorsement
applies, the carrier gives up its subrogation right against
the specified party.

Salvage:
For property claims paid as a total loss, the carrier takes
ownership of the damaged property and sells what value
remains. A totaled vehicle goes to salvage auction; damaged
inventory may be sold to salvage buyers. Salvage proceeds
also reduce net claim cost.

Reserve closure:
As payments are made and recoveries pursued, reserves are
trued up to actual results. Final reserve becomes part of
the closed-file accounting that flows into the carrier's
loss ratio calculation.

Reopening:
A closed claim can be reopened if new facts emerge — a
property loss that turns out to have hidden damage discovered
during repair, a settled liability claim where the claimant
develops complications. Reopen procedures and timeframes
vary by carrier and state.


STAGE 9 — CLAIM CLOSED
=======================

The file closes when:
- All payments have been issued
- Subrogation and salvage recoveries are completed or
  abandoned
- Releases (third-party) or proofs of loss (first-party) are
  on file
- Reserves are reconciled to actual payments and recoveries

Closed claims still affect downstream activity:
- Loss history affects renewal underwriting and pricing
- Loss runs document the claim history for new-carrier
  applications
- Trending and analytics on closed claims feed carrier
  reserving, pricing, and product development


KEY ROLES IN THE CLAIMS PROCESS
================================

Adjuster (staff, independent, or public):
Investigates and evaluates. Different parties — carrier-side
(staff/independent) vs insured-side (public) — have different
loyalties.

Claims examiner / supervisor:
Reviews and approves significant reserve changes, settlement
authority, and coverage decisions. Higher-authority files
escalate up the management chain.

Coverage counsel:
Lawyers retained by the carrier to advise on coverage
analysis, especially for complex policies, novel issues, or
contested ROR situations.

Defense counsel:
Lawyers retained by the carrier to defend the insured in
third-party suits. Defense counsel's primary duty is to the
insured even though paid by the carrier — an area where
ethical rules can create tension in coverage-disputed cases.

Insured:
Has duties to cooperate, provide statements, allow
inspection, produce records, attend EUOs if demanded, and
forward suit papers promptly.

Claimant (third-party claims):
Not a party to the insurance contract but the person seeking
recovery against the insured. The carrier's relationship to
the claimant is adversarial, not fiduciary — but state law
imposes good-faith claims-handling duties even toward
claimants in many jurisdictions.

Public adjuster (first-party):
Optional. Represents the insured in negotiating the claim
value with the carrier. Adds expertise but also adds a fee
out of the recovery.


COMMON CLAIMS-PROCESS DOCUMENTS
================================

- Acknowledgment letter (carrier to insured)
- Recorded statement (insured, claimant, witnesses)
- Examination Under Oath (EUO) transcript
- Reservation of Rights (ROR) letter
- Denial letter
- Proof of Loss (insured to carrier, first-party)
- Sworn statement in proof of loss
- Demand letter (claimant or counsel to carrier)
- Release (settling claimant releases insured and carrier)
- Subrogation demand and pursuit correspondence
- Loss runs (claim history summary for underwriting)


WHEN THE PROCESS GOES SIDEWAYS
===============================

Common failure modes:

Late notice:
Insured delays reporting; carrier asserts prejudice; coverage
gets disputed before substance is even reached.

Failure to cooperate:
Insured refuses to provide statements, attend EUO, or produce
documents; carrier may deny based on breach of cooperation
condition.

Coverage gap discovered at claim time:
The exclusion or endorsement that matters wasn't read until
after the loss. By then the placement decisions are locked.

Reservation of Rights handled poorly:
ROR not issued when it should have been, or issued too
broadly, can either waive the carrier's coverage defenses or
trigger independent counsel obligations.

Settlement authority issues:
Adjuster lacks authority to settle for what the case is worth;
case drifts toward verdict; insured exposed to excess judgment
risk.

Bad-faith claim exposure:
Unreasonable delay, inadequate investigation, wrongful denial,
or failure to settle a within-limits demand can all expose the
carrier to bad-faith liability beyond the policy limit.

The claims process exists to balance prompt indemnification
against accurate coverage determination — and the failure
modes mostly arise when one side of that balance gets
neglected.
