Endorsements: How Policies Get Modified
========================================

This document explains what insurance endorsements are, why they
exist, how they're identified, and the most common types you'll
encounter. Endorsements are the mechanism by which the standard
policy contract gets customized — adding coverage, removing coverage,
adjusting terms, naming additional parties, or complying with state
law requirements.


WHAT IS AN ENDORSEMENT?
=======================

An endorsement is a written amendment to an insurance policy. It
modifies, adds to, or removes part of the standard policy contract.
Once attached, an endorsement becomes part of the policy and is
legally binding — it has the same contractual force as the base
policy form.

Endorsements exist because no two insureds have exactly the same
risk. The standard policy is the starting point; endorsements are
how the carrier (and the agent) tune that starting point to the
specific exposures, requirements, and preferences of an individual
account. A contractor's CGL policy might have ten or more
endorsements; a simple homeowners policy might have just two or
three.

Key principle: endorsements take precedence over the unmodified
policy form. If an endorsement contradicts the base form, the
endorsement controls — because it's the more specific document.


HOW ARE ENDORSEMENTS IDENTIFIED?
================================

Every endorsement carries:
- A form number identifying what the endorsement does
- An edition date showing which version of that endorsement is being
  used
- An effective date showing when it attached to the policy
- Sometimes a schedule listing specific items (additional insureds,
  locations, vehicles, etc.)

Endorsement numbering conventions:
- ISO endorsements use codes like CG 20 10 (Commercial General
  Liability — Additional Insured — Owners, Lessees, or Contractors —
  Scheduled Person or Organization)
- AAIS endorsements use similar conventions
- Carrier-proprietary endorsements use carrier-specific numbering

The form number tells you what the endorsement is FOR. The edition
date tells you which version is on the policy — endorsement wording
changes over time, sometimes materially. A contract requiring "CG 20
10" without specifying an edition leaves room for argument about
whether the carrier issued the right version.


WHO ISSUES ENDORSEMENTS?
========================

Endorsements are issued by the insurance carrier, not the insured.
The insured can REQUEST a change (typically via an ACORD change
request — ACORD 70 for personal non-auto, ACORD 71 for personal
auto, ACORD 175 for commercial), but the carrier writes and attaches
the endorsement that effects the change.

For standardized endorsements (ISO/AAIS), the carrier uses an
off-the-shelf form. For unusual requests, the carrier may issue a
"manuscript" endorsement — custom wording for that specific
situation. Manuscript endorsements get more underwriting scrutiny
because they introduce non-standard language into the contract.


WHEN DO ENDORSEMENTS ATTACH TO A POLICY?
========================================

There are three primary times endorsements get added:

At policy inception:
Some endorsements are part of the initial policy — coverage
extensions selected at quoting, mandatory state amendatory
endorsements, standard package endorsements the carrier always
attaches. These attach effective the policy inception date.

Mid-term:
Changes the insured requests after the policy is bound — adding a
vehicle, adding a mortgagee, increasing a limit, adding an
additional insured for a new contract. The endorsement has a
mid-term effective date and stays in force for the remainder of the
policy period.

At renewal:
The carrier may add, remove, or change endorsements at renewal as
part of underwriting review. Renewal endorsement changes can affect
coverage materially, so renewals warrant a careful comparison of the
expiring policy's endorsements to the renewing policy's
endorsements.


COMMON ENDORSEMENT CATEGORIES
=============================

Additional Insured endorsements:
The most common contractually-required endorsement. Names a third
party (vendor, landlord, customer, general contractor) as an
"additional insured" on the policy, extending coverage to them for
liability arising from the named insured's operations. Common ISO
versions:
- CG 20 10: Additional Insured — Owners, Lessees, or Contractors —
  Scheduled (ongoing operations)
- CG 20 37: Additional Insured — Owners, Lessees, or Contractors —
  Completed Operations (post-completion exposure)
- CG 20 26: Additional Insured — Designated Person or Organization

Different AI endorsement editions provide materially different
coverage. CG 20 10 10 01 is broader than CG 20 10 04 13, for example.
Contracts requiring "additional insured status" without specifying
form and edition can leave room for argument.

Waiver of Subrogation endorsements:
Subrogation is the carrier's right to pursue recovery from a third
party who caused a loss. A "waiver of subrogation" endorsement gives
up that right against a specified party — typically required by
contracts where the parties have agreed not to sue each other.

Primary and Non-Contributory endorsements:
Specifies that the named insured's policy responds first (primary)
and doesn't share the loss with any other policy the additional
insured might have (non-contributory). Standard contractual
requirement on construction and vendor contracts.

Per Project / Per Location Aggregate endorsements:
By default, a CGL policy has a single general aggregate limit shared
across all projects/locations. A per-project (CG 25 03) or
per-location (CG 25 04) aggregate endorsement gives each project or
location its own separate aggregate, multiplying available capacity
for accounts with multiple projects.

Coverage-adding endorsements:
Examples — Water Backup of Sewers and Drains (HO 04 95), Equipment
Breakdown, Identity Theft, Scheduled Personal Property, Earthquake,
Ordinance or Law, Service Line Coverage. These add coverage that
wasn't in the base form.

Coverage-restricting / exclusion endorsements:
Examples — Total Pollution Exclusion, Asbestos Exclusion,
Communicable Disease Exclusion (added widely post-COVID), Lead-Based
Paint Exclusion, Designated Operations Exclusion. These remove
coverage the base form provided.

State amendatory endorsements:
Modify the base policy to comply with state-specific laws. Every
state requires some adjustments — fraud warning language,
cancellation notice requirements, specific definitions. These are
typically mandatory and non-negotiable.

Administrative endorsements:
Named insured changes, address changes, mortgagee changes, loss
payee changes. These don't modify coverage substantively — they
update administrative facts on the policy.


ENDORSEMENTS vs OTHER POLICY COMPONENTS
=======================================

A complete insurance policy is built from several layers:

Declarations page (dec page):
The cover-sheet summary — named insured, policy period, premium,
coverage limits and deductibles, applicable forms and endorsements.
The dec page references everything else but doesn't itself contain
coverage language.

Coverage form:
The substantive contract defining what is and isn't covered (ISO
forms like CG 00 01, CA 00 01, CP 00 10, or AAIS or carrier
equivalents). This is the "base" policy.

Endorsements:
Modifications layered on top of the coverage form. May add, remove,
or change coverage.

Conditions:
Rules that apply to all coverage parts — duties after loss, premium
audit, cancellation procedures, subrogation. Usually built into the
coverage form rather than separate endorsements.

Exclusions:
What the policy doesn't cover. May be in the base form, in
endorsements, or both. Endorsement exclusions often override base
form coverage — for example, the base form might cover pollution
broadly, but a Total Pollution Exclusion endorsement removes most of
that coverage.

Order of reading: a complete coverage analysis requires reading the
declarations to identify what's attached, then the coverage form for
the baseline, then each endorsement in turn to see what's been
added, removed, or modified.


WHEN AN ENDORSEMENT GOES WRONG
==============================

Common endorsement issues:
- Wrong form number issued (contract required CG 20 10, carrier
  issued CG 20 33)
- Wrong edition issued (contract required CG 20 10 10 01, carrier
  issued CG 20 10 04 13 with materially narrower coverage)
- Effective date mismatch (endorsement effective the day after the
  loss it was supposed to cover)
- Missing schedule (Additional Insured endorsement requires a
  schedule listing the AI's name; blank schedule = no coverage)
- Conflicting endorsements (two endorsements on the same policy
  saying different things; courts generally read them together but
  ambiguity favors the insured)

Compare the endorsement language against the contractual
requirement. Don't assume the form number alone is sufficient —
verify the edition date and any required schedule entries.


HOW ENDORSEMENTS GET TRACKED
============================

The Forms and Endorsements Schedule (ACORD 829) is the policy's
roadmap — it lists every form and endorsement attached, with form
numbers, edition dates, and titles. For coverage analysis after a
loss or at renewal, the ACORD 829 (or carrier equivalent) is the
starting point: identify what's on the policy, then read each piece.

Most carriers also maintain endorsement issuance tracking internally
so endorsements can be reissued, voided, or amended as needed.
