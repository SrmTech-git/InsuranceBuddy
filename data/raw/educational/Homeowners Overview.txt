Homeowners Insurance: Policy Structure and Coverage
=====================================================

This document explains how homeowners policies are structured, what
the standard forms cover, the key coverage parts, common
endorsements, and what's typically excluded. Homeowners is a package
policy — it combines property and liability coverage for a residence
into a single contract.


WHAT A HOMEOWNERS POLICY IS
===========================

A homeowners (HO) policy is a package contract covering:
- The dwelling itself
- Other structures on the premises (detached garage, shed, fence)
- Personal property (contents)
- Loss of use (additional living expense when the home is
  uninhabitable)
- Personal liability (lawsuits arising from the insured's
  negligence)
- Medical payments to others (no-fault payments for injuries on
  premises)

The owner-occupant is the typical named insured. Resident family
members are usually covered automatically. Tenants, roommates, and
non-resident family members are NOT covered unless specifically
added.


STANDARD HO FORMS
=================

ISO publishes a family of homeowners forms, each tailored to a
different occupancy or coverage scope:

HO-2 (Broad Form):
Named-perils policy. Covers the dwelling and personal property only
against perils specifically listed (fire, lightning, windstorm,
hail, explosion, riot, vehicles, smoke, vandalism, theft, falling
objects, weight of ice/snow, accidental water discharge, freezing,
artificially generated electricity, volcanic eruption). If a peril
isn't listed, it isn't covered.

HO-3 (Special Form):
The most common homeowners form. The dwelling and other structures
are covered on an open-perils ("all-risk") basis — covered for any
cause of loss not specifically excluded. Personal property is
covered on a named-perils basis (the HO-2 list). HO-3 is the
default for most owner-occupied single-family homes.

HO-5 (Comprehensive Form):
Like HO-3, but personal property is ALSO covered on an open-perils
basis. Broader than HO-3, costs a bit more. Typical for higher-
value homes or insureds who want maximum personal property coverage.

HO-4 (Contents Broad / Renters):
For tenants. Covers personal property, liability, loss of use, and
medical payments. Does NOT cover the building (that's the
landlord's responsibility). See the separate Renters and Condo
overview.

HO-6 (Unit-Owners / Condo):
For condominium owners. Covers personal property, liability, loss
of use, and improvements/betterments inside the unit. The building
shell is covered by the condo association's master policy. See the
Renters and Condo overview.

HO-8 (Modified Form):
For older homes where replacement cost would significantly exceed
market value. Pays on a modified actual cash value basis. Named-
perils coverage similar to HO-2 but narrower (no theft of property
away from premises, for example).

DP forms (Dwelling Property):
A separate family for non-owner-occupied dwellings — rental
properties, seasonal homes, vacant dwellings. DP-1, DP-2, DP-3
parallel HO-2/3 in scope.


COVERAGE PARTS A THROUGH F
==========================

Standard homeowners policies use a Coverage A/B/C/D/E/F structure:

Coverage A — Dwelling:
The main structure, attached structures (attached garage, attached
deck), built-in fixtures, and materials on premises intended for
use in construction. Limit selected at quoting based on replacement
cost.

Coverage B — Other Structures:
Detached structures (detached garage, shed, fence, gazebo, in-
ground pool structures). Default limit is typically 10% of Coverage
A; can be increased by endorsement.

Coverage C — Personal Property:
Contents — furniture, clothing, electronics, appliances. Default
limit is typically 50%-70% of Coverage A. Includes coverage for
property anywhere in the world (subject to sub-limits for property
away from premises). Sub-limits apply to specific categories:
jewelry, watches, furs, firearms, silverware, business property,
cash. To exceed sub-limits, schedule items individually.

Coverage D — Loss of Use:
Additional living expense (ALE) when the home is uninhabitable due
to a covered loss — hotel, restaurant meals over normal grocery
costs, temporary rental. Also covers fair rental value if part of
the home was rented out. Default limit is typically 20%-30% of
Coverage A. Sometimes paid on an actual-loss basis with a time
limit instead of a dollar cap.

Coverage E — Personal Liability:
Pays for bodily injury or property damage the insured is legally
liable for. Defense costs are typically in addition to the limit.
Common limit options: $100K, $300K, $500K, $1M. Excludes business,
auto, intentional acts, and certain other exposures.

Coverage F — Medical Payments to Others:
No-fault payments for medical expenses of someone injured on the
premises or by the insured's activities, regardless of liability.
Doesn't cover the insured or resident family members. Typical
limits: $1K-$5K per person.


REPLACEMENT COST vs ACTUAL CASH VALUE
======================================

Coverage A (dwelling) on HO-3 and HO-5 is typically written on a
replacement cost basis: the carrier pays to rebuild without
deduction for depreciation, subject to the policy limit. To
collect full replacement cost, the insured usually has to actually
rebuild — otherwise payment is on an actual cash value (ACV) basis
first, with replacement cost holdback paid after rebuilding.

Coverage C (personal property) defaults to ACV — replacement cost
minus depreciation — but can be upgraded to replacement cost via
endorsement. Most modern homeowners policies sold today include
replacement cost on contents as a standard feature, but read the
declarations to confirm.

Coinsurance / insurance-to-value:
Most homeowners forms require the dwelling to be insured to at
least 80% of replacement cost. If under-insured below that
threshold, a coinsurance penalty applies at partial loss — the
carrier pays only the proportion of the loss equal to (amount
carried / amount required). Insurance-to-value is enforced
because partial losses are far more common than total losses.


INFLATION GUARD AND EXTENDED REPLACEMENT COST
=============================================

Inflation guard endorsement:
Automatically increases the Coverage A limit periodically (often
quarterly or annually) to keep pace with construction cost
inflation. Common on most modern HO policies.

Extended replacement cost:
Pays a percentage above the Coverage A limit (often 25%-50%) if
rebuilding costs exceed the policy limit — protection against
under-insurance from rapid cost spikes.

Guaranteed replacement cost:
Pays whatever it costs to rebuild, even above the policy limit.
Increasingly rare due to cost spikes; often subject to
underwriting restrictions (recent inspection, home in good
condition, accurate Coverage A from a replacement cost estimator).


COMMON HO ENDORSEMENTS
======================

Water Backup of Sewers and Drains (HO 04 95):
Covers damage from water backing up through sewers, drains, or
sump pump failure. NOT covered by the base HO-3 — must be added.
Common claim type, often added by default at modest premium.

Scheduled Personal Property (HO 04 61):
Schedules specific high-value items (jewelry, art, firearms,
collectibles, musical instruments) individually with appraised
values. Removes Coverage C sub-limits for those items, often
covers on an open-perils basis including mysterious disappearance.

Ordinance or Law (HO 04 77):
Covers the increased cost to rebuild to current building code when
repairing a damaged home that doesn't meet current code. Base
policy excludes the "code upgrade" portion of repair costs.

Identity Theft Coverage (HO 04 55):
Covers expenses associated with restoring identity after theft —
attorney fees, lost wages, notarization, mailing.

Service Line Coverage:
Covers damage to underground utility lines (water, sewer, gas,
electrical, fiber) running from the street to the home — base
policy excludes these.

Equipment Breakdown:
Covers mechanical breakdown of major systems (HVAC, water heater,
electrical panel) — base policy excludes "mechanical breakdown."

Earthquake Endorsement:
Earthquake is excluded from base HO. Endorsement adds coverage,
typically with a separate (percentage-based) deductible.

Animal Liability:
Some carriers exclude or sub-limit dog bites (especially for
certain breeds). Endorsement may be required to include or
restore.

Home-Sharing / Short-Term Rental:
Standard HO excludes "business" use, which courts have applied to
Airbnb/VRBO income. Endorsement adds short-term rental coverage.


COMMON EXCLUSIONS
=================

Standard HO exclusions include:
- Flood (separate NFIP or private flood policy required)
- Earthquake (separate endorsement or policy)
- War and nuclear hazard
- Wear and tear, deterioration, mechanical breakdown
- Mold (often capped or excluded; can sometimes be endorsed back
  with sub-limits)
- Intentional acts by the insured
- Business pursuits (with narrow exceptions for incidental home
  business)
- Motor vehicles (except riding mowers, golf carts, etc. with
  conditions)
- Aircraft and watercraft above sub-limits
- Loss to the dwelling caused by vacancy beyond a stated period
  (typically 60 days)
- Earth movement, sinkhole (in some states; separate endorsement
  may be available)


DEDUCTIBLES
===========

Per-occurrence dollar deductibles ($500, $1,000, $2,500, $5,000)
are standard. Some perils have separate deductibles:

- Wind/hail deductible: in catastrophe-prone states (FL, TX, OK,
  coastal states), wind/hail often carries a percentage deductible
  (1%-5% of Coverage A) instead of the flat deductible.
- Hurricane deductible: in named-storm coastal states, a separate
  percentage hurricane deductible triggers when a storm is named.
- Earthquake deductible: typically 10%-25% of the building limit.


MORTGAGEE AND ADDITIONAL INTERESTS
===================================

Mortgagee clause: if the home has a mortgage, the lender is named
as mortgagee. The mortgagee has rights independent of the insured
— including the right to receive notice of cancellation and to
collect on loss payments up to its insurable interest. Mortgagees
require evidence of insurance (ACORD 27) at closing and annually.

Additional insured: less common on HO but sometimes used for an
ex-spouse with a financial interest, a co-owner, or an estate.


WHEN AN HO POLICY IS NOT THE RIGHT FIT
=======================================

- Rental properties: use Dwelling Property (DP-1/2/3) instead.
- Tenants: use HO-4 (renters).
- Condo unit owners: use HO-6.
- Vacant homes awaiting sale: use a Vacant Dwelling policy or
  specialty form — most HO policies suspend or limit coverage
  after 30-60 days of vacancy.
- Mobile/manufactured homes: use a Mobile Homeowners form.
- High-value homes ($2M+ replacement cost): consider a high-value
  carrier (Chubb, AIG Private Client, PURE, Cincinnati Executive)
  with cash-out / agreed value provisions.
- Seasonal / secondary homes: may need a Seasonal Dwelling form,
  often with vacancy provisions.
