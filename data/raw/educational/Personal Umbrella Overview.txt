Personal Umbrella Insurance: Excess Liability for Individuals
==============================================================

This document explains what a personal umbrella policy (PUP) is,
how it relates to underlying primary policies, what it covers
beyond underlying, and when an insured should carry one.


WHAT A PERSONAL UMBRELLA IS
============================

A personal umbrella policy is an excess liability contract that
sits on top of underlying primary policies (auto, homeowners,
sometimes watercraft and recreational vehicle policies). Its job
is twofold:

1. Provide additional limits when an underlying policy is
   exhausted — pay the portion of a liability claim that exceeds
   the underlying limit, up to the umbrella's own limit.
2. Provide broader coverage than the underlying — fill certain
   coverage gaps the underlying excludes (with conditions; see
   below).

Common umbrella limits: $1M, $2M, $3M, $5M, $10M. The most common
size for an individual is $1M-$2M; high-net-worth individuals
commonly carry $5M+.

Premium is typically modest relative to the coverage — a $1M
umbrella for a typical household often costs $200-$500/year
because it sits behind underlying limits and the carrier expects
claims that high to be rare.


HOW THE UMBRELLA ATTACHES TO UNDERLYING
========================================

Umbrellas require the insured to maintain "underlying limits"
specified in the umbrella's declarations. Typical underlying
requirements:

- Personal auto: 250/500/100 BI/PD (or CSL $500K)
- Personal auto UM/UIM: matching auto BI limit, often required
- Homeowners liability: $300K-$500K
- Watercraft liability (if applicable): $300K-$500K
- Recreational vehicles (ATV, motorcycle, golf cart, snowmobile):
  varies

If the insured drops below the required underlying, the umbrella
treats them as if the required limit was still in place — meaning
the insured personally absorbs the gap between actual underlying
and required underlying. This is sometimes called the "self-
insured retention by default" mechanic.

If the insured maintains underlying as required, the umbrella
attaches immediately above the underlying limit with no gap.


SELF-INSURED RETENTION (SIR)
=============================

When the umbrella covers something the underlying does NOT cover
at all (a coverage broader than underlying), the umbrella requires
the insured to satisfy a self-insured retention — typically $250
or $1,000 — out of pocket before the umbrella pays. The SIR
functions like a deductible for the umbrella-only exposures.

Example: an umbrella covers worldwide personal injury including
libel and slander. The auto and HO policies don't cover libel/
slander. If the insured is sued for defamation, the umbrella pays
above the SIR amount.


WHAT AN UMBRELLA COVERS THAT UNDERLYING USUALLY DOESN'T
========================================================

Personal injury (in the liability sense):
Standard auto and HO cover "bodily injury" and "property damage."
Umbrellas often add "personal injury" — libel, slander,
defamation, false arrest, invasion of privacy, malicious
prosecution. This is the most commonly cited broader coverage.

Worldwide coverage:
Some auto/HO policies have geographic limitations. Umbrellas
typically cover liability worldwide.

Landlord activities:
If the insured rents out a property (a vacation home, a single
rental unit), the umbrella often extends to that exposure if
underlying DP/rental dwelling coverage is in place. Excessive
rental operations move the insured into commercial territory.

Volunteer / nonprofit board service:
Some umbrellas extend to "incidental" volunteer work or unpaid
board service for nonprofits. NOT a substitute for D&O — for
paid board service or material exposure, get D&O.


WHAT UMBRELLAS TYPICALLY EXCLUDE
=================================

- Business pursuits (most umbrellas are personal-only; business
  activities require Commercial Umbrella)
- Professional services (need separate E&O / professional
  liability)
- Aircraft (often excluded; some umbrellas allow scheduled
  aircraft for additional premium)
- Watercraft above certain size/horsepower (often $500K-$1M
  underlying watercraft policy required; large yachts excluded)
- Intentional acts
- Criminal acts
- Workers compensation obligations (if the insured is liable as
  an employer)
- Damage to property in the insured's care, custody, or control
- Auto racing / speed contests
- Discrimination (varies by carrier; some umbrellas exclude
  entirely, others have sub-limits)


DROP-DOWN vs FOLLOW-FORM
=========================

Drop-down umbrella:
If the underlying primary is exhausted by other claims (so the
limit is depleted before this claim attaches), the umbrella
"drops down" and replaces the underlying's coverage from dollar
one of this claim (subject to SIR). Not all umbrellas drop down
— some require the underlying to still be in force.

Follow-form umbrella:
The umbrella adopts the underlying's coverage definitions and
exclusions. If underlying covers it, the umbrella covers it
(subject to umbrella exclusions). If underlying excludes it, the
umbrella likely also excludes it.

Most personal umbrellas are a hybrid: follow-form for most
exposures, with a defined broader-than-underlying scope for
personal injury and a few other categories.


WHO IS COVERED
==============

The named insured and resident family members, usually with the
same definition as the underlying HO and PAP.

Some umbrellas extend coverage to non-resident college students,
nannies/domestic employees on duty, and other narrowly defined
groups — read the umbrella's "insured" definition carefully.


WHEN TO CARRY AN UMBRELLA
=========================

Common triggers for recommending umbrella:
- Net worth above underlying limits — anyone with assets a
  plaintiff could reach beyond auto/HO limits should consider
  umbrella
- Teenage drivers in the household (high accident frequency,
  often serious injuries)
- Swimming pool, trampoline, dog of a breed associated with
  bite claims, or other "attractive nuisance" exposures
- Frequent entertaining at the residence
- Boat ownership above small-craft size
- Rental properties (modest rental exposure; large landlord
  portfolios need commercial umbrella)
- Volunteer board service for nonprofits
- High-profile or high-income individuals at greater risk of
  large-claim litigation
- Anyone in a profession where social-media or reputational
  exposure could lead to defamation claims

Industry rule of thumb: carry umbrella limits at least equal to
total assets plus a margin for future earnings. Plaintiffs collect
against assets and wages — the umbrella exists to protect them.


UMBRELLA vs EXCESS LIABILITY
=============================

The terms "umbrella" and "excess" are sometimes used
interchangeably, but technically:

- Umbrella: provides excess limits AND broader-than-underlying
  coverage (with SIR for the broader piece). What's standard in
  the personal market.
- True Excess: provides excess limits only — follows underlying
  exactly, no broader coverage. More common in commercial. Less
  expensive than an umbrella because it doesn't add coverage.

Most personal "umbrella" products do include the broader
coverage piece, even when marketed simply as excess liability.


CARRIER UMBRELLA vs STANDALONE
===============================

Two common structures:

Companion umbrella:
Written by the same carrier as the underlying auto/HO. Simpler
because the carrier already knows the underlying. Often slightly
cheaper. Usually requires the insured to also carry their
auto/HO with that carrier.

Standalone umbrella:
Written by a specialty carrier (Chubb, RLI, Cincinnati, etc.)
separately from auto/HO. More flexibility — the insured can
shop auto/HO without losing the umbrella. Useful for high-net-
worth households with unusual underlying carriers, or for
insureds whose auto/HO carrier doesn't offer adequate umbrella
limits.


CLAIMS AND DEFENSE
==================

When a claim exceeds underlying limits (or hits an umbrella-only
exposure):
1. The underlying carrier defends and pays up to its limit.
2. When underlying limits are exhausted (or for umbrella-only
   exposures, after the SIR), the umbrella takes over defense
   and payment.
3. Defense costs are typically IN ADDITION to the umbrella limit
   for follow-form claims, but check the policy — some umbrellas
   include defense within the limit.
4. The insured must cooperate with the umbrella carrier's
   investigation and defense, same as with the underlying.


WHEN UMBRELLA ISN'T THE RIGHT FIT
==================================

- Pure business exposure → Commercial Umbrella
- Pure professional exposure → Professional Liability / E&O
- Watercraft above modest size → Yacht policy with high
  liability limits (umbrella as excess on top)
- Aviation exposure → Aircraft hull and liability policy, with
  aviation umbrella as needed
- D&O for paid board service or significant nonprofit
  governance → D&O policy, not personal umbrella
