Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI)
================================================

This document explains what EPLI covers, the kinds of employment
claims it responds to, who is insured, the common exclusions,
and how EPLI relates to other management liability coverages
like D&O and Fiduciary.


WHAT EPLI COVERS
=================

EPLI covers the employer's liability arising from employment
practices — claims by employees, former employees, and
applicants alleging wrongful treatment in the employment
relationship.

Why standalone EPLI exists:
General liability and workers compensation don't cover
employment claims:
- CGL excludes employment-related practices via the
  "employment-related practices" exclusion
- Workers comp covers physical workplace injuries, not
  discrimination, harassment, or wrongful termination

EPLI emerged as a distinct coverage in the 1990s as
employment litigation grew — protected-class discrimination
suits, sexual harassment claims, ADA claims, retaliation
claims. The market has expanded to cover a wide range of
employment-related exposures.


COVERED CLAIM TYPES
====================

Standard EPLI policies cover:

Discrimination:
Claims alleging adverse employment action based on a
protected characteristic — race, color, national origin,
sex, age, religion, disability, pregnancy, sexual
orientation, gender identity, veteran status, genetic
information, and others (federal under Title VII, ADEA, ADA,
GINA, etc., plus state and local protected classes that
extend further).

Harassment:
Hostile work environment claims, sexual harassment, and
harassment based on any protected characteristic. Both
"quid pro quo" and "hostile work environment" theories
typically covered.

Wrongful termination:
Claims that a termination violated law (discrimination,
retaliation, public policy violations) or contract (implied
contract claims, severance disputes). At-will employment
limits these in many states, but exceptions to at-will
exist in every jurisdiction.

Retaliation:
Claims that the employer retaliated against an employee for
engaging in protected activity — complaining about
discrimination, filing a workers comp claim, reporting
safety violations (OSHA), whistleblowing on financial
fraud, refusing to participate in illegal conduct.

Failure to hire / failure to promote:
Discrimination claims from job applicants or current
employees passed over for promotion based on protected
characteristics.

Constructive discharge:
Resignation claims where the employee alleges the work
environment was made so intolerable by the employer's
discrimination, harassment, or retaliation that no
reasonable person could continue — treated legally as a
termination.

Workplace defamation, invasion of privacy:
Claims arising from reference statements about former
employees, invasion of workplace privacy (electronic
monitoring claims), or defamatory internal communications.

Negligent hiring / supervision / retention:
Claims by third parties (sometimes employees, sometimes
outsiders) that the employer knew or should have known about
an employee's dangerous propensities. Coverage scope varies
by policy.

Wage and hour:
Largely EXCLUDED from standard EPLI or sub-limited heavily.
Class actions under FLSA and state wage laws have become
some of the most expensive employment exposures, and most
carriers won't write full coverage for them. Defense-only
coverage with sub-limits is sometimes available.

Third-party harassment / discrimination:
Some EPLI policies extend coverage to claims by NON-employees
(customers, vendors, visitors) alleging discrimination or
harassment by the insured's employees. Important for
consumer-facing businesses.


WHO IS INSURED
==============

The named insured (the employer entity) and:
- Directors, officers, partners, members (in their capacity
  as employees or supervisors)
- Employees (when acting within scope of employment)
- Former employees (for acts during their employment)
- Often: independent contractors, when the policy includes
  "leased employees" or similar extensions

WHO IS A CLAIMANT (the people who can sue):
- Current employees
- Former employees
- Applicants for employment
- Third parties (for third-party harassment / discrimination
  if the coverage extension applies)
- Independent contractors and temporary workers (depending
  on policy and on the legal status — joint employer claims
  blur this line)


CLAIMS-MADE COVERAGE
=====================

EPLI is universally claims-made. Same mechanics as E&O and
D&O:

- Retroactive date — acts before the retro date aren't
  covered
- Tail / Extended Reporting Period — extends reporting
  window after termination
- Prior acts coverage — preserves coverage for prior work
  when starting a new policy

Specific to employment: claims often follow termination or
adverse action by months or years. An employee fired in 2024
may file an EEOC charge in early 2025, get a right-to-sue
letter, and file suit later in 2025 — meaning the claim hits
the carrier in force at the time the EEOC charge or suit is
filed, not the carrier in force at termination. Retro date
and tail provisions need to align with this timing pattern.


DEFENSE COSTS
==============

Defense is usually WITHIN limits on EPLI — defense erodes
what's available for settlement. Employment litigation is
expensive (depositions, document discovery, expert witnesses,
class certification battles for class actions), and defense
costs commonly consume a significant portion of the limit on
contested cases.

Counsel selection:
Some EPLI policies let the carrier select defense counsel;
others let the insured choose from a carrier panel; some
allow free choice of counsel subject to carrier billing
rates. Larger and more sophisticated insureds negotiate
counsel selection rights.

Settlement consent:
Most policies require carrier consent to settle. "Hammer
clause" provisions cap the carrier's liability if the
insured refuses to settle within limits when the carrier
recommends settlement — limiting the carrier's exposure to
what would have been paid if the recommended settlement
had been accepted.

EEOC and administrative agency proceedings:
EEOC charges, state human rights commission charges, and
similar administrative proceedings typically trigger
defense coverage before any lawsuit is filed. Some policies
cover administrative defense as a separate sub-limit; others
fold it into the main limit.


COMMON EPLI EXCLUSIONS
=======================

Wage and hour:
As noted, largely excluded or sub-limited. The FLSA and
state wage law class action exposure has been a major
underwriting issue.

Bodily injury and property damage:
Belongs on CGL.

Workers compensation:
Belongs on WC. EPLI excludes claims that are statutorily
within the workers comp system.

Breach of contract:
Some breach-of-employment-contract claims are excluded;
some are covered (carve-back for "wrongful termination"
including breach of implied contract). Read the specific
policy.

ERISA / employee benefits:
Belongs on Fiduciary Liability.

Acts of insureds:
Intentional discrimination by senior management may be
excluded, with severability so innocent insureds retain
coverage. "Knowing wrongdoing" exclusions typically require
final adjudication.

Strike, lockout, labor disputes:
Labor relations claims often excluded.

WARN Act:
Federal and state plant-closing/mass-layoff notification
violations may be excluded or sub-limited.

Immigration / I-9 claims:
Often excluded.

Specific named matters:
Pending or threatened matters identified at underwriting.

Prior acts / prior known matters:
Standard claims-made exclusion.

OSHA citations and fines:
Regulatory penalties from OSHA typically excluded as
uninsurable (though defense costs sometimes covered).


EPLI AND THE MANAGEMENT LIABILITY SUITE
=========================================

EPLI is one of the four core management liability coverages:
- D&O (governance and securities liability)
- EPLI (employment claims)
- Fiduciary Liability (ERISA and benefits plan governance)
- Crime / Fidelity (employee theft, social engineering)

Private companies and nonprofits often buy these as a
package on a single combined form ("management liability"
package). Public companies typically buy D&O standalone and
the others separately or in smaller packages.

Coordination issues:
- A claim by a senior executive alleging wrongful
  termination AND breach of fiduciary duty by the board can
  trigger BOTH EPLI and D&O. Allocation between the policies
  matters.
- A retaliation claim by a whistleblower who reported
  financial fraud can implicate EPLI (retaliation) AND D&O
  (the underlying fraud allegation against the board).
  Coordination provisions and "other insurance" clauses
  determine which responds first.
- Discrimination claims from senior executives can hit both
  EPLI and D&O depending on policy structure.


COMMON RIDERS AND ENDORSEMENTS
================================

Third-party EPLI:
Extends to claims by non-employees (customers, clients,
vendors). Important for retail, hospitality, healthcare,
education.

Wage and hour defense:
Limited coverage for defense of wage/hour class actions, with
sub-limits and often coinsurance. Indemnity for FLSA
violations remains largely uninsurable.

Immigration practices liability:
Adds I-9 audit defense and limited immigration practices
coverage.

ESL coaching / pre-litigation consulting:
Some EPLI carriers provide free or discounted access to
employment law hotlines and HR consulting services — value-
added services that help prevent claims and structure
responses.


HOW EPLI UNDERWRITING WORKS
============================

Underwriters look at:
- Employee count, industry, and geographic distribution
  (some states are more litigious than others — California,
  New York, New Jersey, Illinois, Massachusetts, DC are
  often noted)
- Employee turnover rate
- Existence and quality of written employment policies
  (handbook, anti-harassment policy, complaint procedures,
  arbitration agreements)
- HR function (in-house counsel, professional HR staff, EAP)
- Training programs (anti-harassment training, manager
  training, documented training records)
- Prior claims history
- Recent reductions in force, plant closures, or
  reorganizations
- Pending or threatened matters

Workforce composition and industry-specific risks (healthcare
patient interaction, retail customer interaction) factor in.
Underwriting questionnaires can be detailed.


COMMON CLAIMS SCENARIOS
========================

- Terminated employee files EEOC charge alleging age
  discrimination after position is eliminated in
  restructuring
- Female employee alleges hostile work environment after
  reporting harassment was met with retaliation
- Class action under FLSA alleging misclassification of
  workers as exempt rather than non-exempt
- ADA claim from employee with disability alleging failure
  to accommodate
- Pregnancy discrimination claim from employee terminated
  shortly after disclosing pregnancy
- Failure-to-hire claim from rejected applicant alleging
  discriminatory interview process
- Constructive discharge claim from employee who resigned
  after being subjected to discriminatory comments by
  supervisor
- Whistleblower retaliation claim from employee who
  reported safety violations to OSHA and was subsequently
  terminated


WHO BUYS EPLI
==============

Most employers benefit from EPLI. The risk-justifying
factors:

- Employee count above a small threshold (some carriers
  decline below 10-25 employees due to spread of risk; the
  small market has expanded over time)
- Industry — healthcare, retail, hospitality, technology,
  financial services often have heightened exposures
- Geographic concentration — operating in employee-favorable
  jurisdictions
- Workforce profile — high turnover, diverse workforce,
  recent reductions in force, customer-facing employees
- Specific risk events — recent terminations, ongoing
  reorganizations, recent acquisitions integrating
  workforces with different cultures
- Contractual requirements — increasingly, business
  partners and customers require their vendors to carry
  EPLI

Small businesses and nonprofits historically underbought
EPLI; the market has expanded coverage availability and
affordability, and EPLI is now common at most size tiers.


KEY TAKEAWAYS
=============

- EPLI covers employment-related claims — discrimination,
  harassment, wrongful termination, retaliation — that CGL
  and WC don't cover
- Wage and hour is largely excluded or heavily sub-limited;
  the most expensive exposure carriers won't fully insure
- Claims-made structure with retro date and tail mechanics;
  timing aligns with EEOC charge / lawsuit filing, not
  termination
- Defense costs typically within limits — eroding limits is
  a real consideration on contested cases
- Sits alongside D&O, Fiduciary, and Crime in management
  liability programs; coordination across policies matters
- Third-party harassment/discrimination extensions important
  for consumer-facing businesses
- Underwriting focuses on workforce profile, HR
  infrastructure, training, and prior claims history
