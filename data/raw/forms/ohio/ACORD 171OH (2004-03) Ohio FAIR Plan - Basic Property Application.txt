ACORD 171OH (2004-03) — Ohio FAIR Plan – Basic Property Application
====================================================================
Form number: ACORD 171OH
Edition: 2004-03
Title: Ohio FAIR Plan – Basic Property Application
Type: Insurance form (ACORD industry standard, Ohio state variant)
States: OH
Policy term: 12 months (typical for FAIR Plan property)
Transaction types: New business (FAIR Plan submission); renewal; midterm change

Purpose:
Master application form for all Ohio FAIR Plan property submissions.
Captures the core property and applicant information common to every
property type; property-type-specific details are captured on the
appropriate supplement (172-177 OH). The Ohio FAIR Plan is Ohio's
state-mandated residual property insurance market administered by
the Ohio FAIR Plan Underwriting Association.

Captures:
- Applicant / owner information (name, address, contact)
- Property location and address
- Property description: construction type, year built, square footage,
  occupancy class, number of stories, roof type, heating type,
  protection class (PPC)
- Building values (replacement cost or actual cash value)
- Contents values
- Coverage limits requested
- Mortgagee / loss payee information
- Prior insurance carrier(s), denial date(s), and reasons for denial
- Loss history (typically 5 years)
- Ohio FAIR Plan statutory disclosures and acknowledgments
- Applicant signature and date

When used:
- New Ohio FAIR Plan submission for any property type — submitted
  together with the applicable supplement (172-177 OH)
- Renewal of existing Ohio FAIR Plan coverage
- Reporting property changes that affect coverage

Notes:
- 171OH is always used in combination with one or more property-type
  supplements:
  * 172OH (Homeowners), 173OH (Dwelling Fire), 174OH (Commercial),
    175OH (Farm), 176OH (Rehabilitation), 177OH (Bowling Alley)
- Ohio FAIR Plan typically provides named-perils coverage (fire +
  extended coverage + VMM); coverage scope depends on the property
  type and selected limits
- Coverage is typically more expensive and more limited than the
  voluntary market — designed as last-resort coverage
